Chapter 03 · 1500 → 1800

First Acceleration

Ships, guns, books, and ledgers — how a handful of small European states used technology to bootstrap the first global economy, and what it cost everyone else.

~38 min read 13 sections 1500 → 1800 AD

The ships that changed everything

In 1492, Columbus's three ships — the Niña, Pinta, and Santa María — were between 50 and 100 tonnes displacement. By the standards of the time they were small, uncomfortable, and technically unremarkable. The Chinese treasure ships of Admiral Zheng He's voyages of 1405–1433 were estimated at 1,500 tonnes — fifteen times larger, more stable, better navigated, better provisioned. Zheng He's fleet reached East Africa decades before any European ship rounded the Cape. By every measure, it was the more impressive achievement.

And yet it was Columbus's small, rickety ships that changed history. Zheng He's voyages were state enterprises, funded and directed by the Ming emperor — and when the next emperor decided to stop them, they stopped. Columbus was a private venture backed by the Spanish crown with profit motive built in from the start. His contract with Ferdinand and Isabella guaranteed him 10% of all revenues from the lands he discovered. The technology was similar. The institutional context was entirely different.

European ships of the late 15th century were genuinely innovative in one key respect: the combination of the lateen sail (allowing sailing closer to the wind, enabling return voyages against prevailing winds) with the square sail (efficient for running downwind) gave them the ability to make round trips across the Atlantic. Without the ability to return, discovery is just disappearance. The round trip made commerce possible.

Why Europe sailed west

In 1453, the Ottomans conquered Constantinople and took control of the eastern trade routes to Asia — the routes that had supplied Europe with spices, silk, and luxury goods. The spice trade was not a luxury. Pepper, cinnamon, cloves, and nutmeg were essential for preserving food before refrigeration. Control of those routes meant control of a substantial portion of the European economy.

European merchants and monarchs desperately needed an alternative. Portugal systematically worked down the African coast from the 1420s onward, seeking a route around Africa to the Indian Ocean. Spain, having just completed the Reconquista in 1492, was looking for its own route. Columbus's proposal — go west to reach the east — was a technological solution to a geopolitical and economic problem. He was wrong about the distance (he massively underestimated the circumference of the earth) but accidentally right about there being land where he thought the Indies were.

The 360° context
Europe in 1492
Europe · 1492

Geopolitical: Europe was a patchwork of competing states — no unified empire, constant warfare. Competition between states meant no single authority could shut down exploration. Portugal and Spain were in explicit competition; England and France would follow.

Economic: The Ottoman closure of eastern trade routes had disrupted the Mediterranean economy. The Italian city-states that had dominated European finance were losing ground. Atlantic-facing states — Portugal, Spain, England, the Netherlands — were about to replace them.

Energy: Wind power for ships was effectively free and unlimited. The constraint was navigational knowledge and ship design, both of which had been steadily improving for decades.

Political: The Spanish crown had just unified Castile and Aragon through marriage, expelled the Moors and Jews, and was looking for both revenue and a crusading mission. Columbus's proposal fitted both needs.

Columbus and the consequence

Columbus landed in the Bahamas on 12 October 1492. He found people, gold in small quantities, and evidence of larger civilisations to the west. He returned to Spain with samples of both, and the second voyage was far larger — seventeen ships, 1,200 men, livestock and seeds for colonisation.

What followed over the next century was one of the most catastrophic series of events in human history, and also one of the most economically transformative. The indigenous populations of the Americas were devastated by European diseases — smallpox, measles, influenza — to which they had no immunity. Estimates of mortality vary widely, but the most careful scholarship suggests that the population of the Americas fell from perhaps 50–60 million in 1492 to around 6 million by 1600. This was not primarily the result of direct violence, though there was plenty of that — it was the result of epidemic disease moving faster than any army.

The moral dimension of what happened is important but not the focus of this chapter. The technological and economic dimension is: the conquest of the Americas gave European states access to resources — land, labour (enslaved), silver, new crops — on a scale that transformed the global economy and funded the technological development that led to industrialisation.

The Columbian Exchange

The movement of plants, animals, and diseases between the Old and New Worlds — what the historian Alfred Crosby called the Columbian Exchange — was arguably the single most important ecological event since the last Ice Age.

From the Americas to Europe and Asia went: potatoes, maize (corn), tomatoes, cacao, tobacco, rubber, and a dozen other crops. The potato alone had an extraordinary effect on European nutrition — it produces more calories per acre than almost any cereal grain, grows in marginal soils, and stores well. The Irish population quadrupled in the century after the potato's adoption, fuelled almost entirely by the new crop. The same pattern happened across Europe and into China, where the sweet potato enabled cultivation of hillside soils that had previously been worthless.

From Europe to the Americas went: horses, cattle, sheep, pigs, wheat, smallpox, measles, and influenza. The horses transformed the cultures of the North American plains within a century — indigenous peoples who adopted the horse became highly mobile, effective hunters and warriors. The livestock transformed the ecology of the Americas irreversibly. The diseases killed tens of millions.

Technology as accident

The Columbian Exchange was not planned. Nobody set out to transform global agriculture by moving crops between continents. The consequences — the Irish population boom, the transformation of Italian cuisine by the tomato, the Chinese expansion into previously marginal land — emerged from millions of individual decisions by farmers, merchants, and sailors responding to local incentives. The largest technological transformations are often not the products of deliberate design but of systemic emergent effects from many small decisions.

Silver and the first global economy

In 1545, Spanish explorers discovered Cerro Rico — the Rich Mountain — at Potosí in modern Bolivia. It was, essentially, a mountain of silver ore. Over the following 150 years, approximately 150,000 tonnes of silver were extracted and shipped to Spain, with devastating consequences for the indigenous workers forced to mine it and paradoxical consequences for Spain itself.

The silver created the first genuinely global economy. It didn't stay in Spain — it flowed immediately toward Asia to pay for the Chinese silk and Indian spices that Europe desperately wanted. Chinese merchants, who priced goods in silver, were the ultimate destination for roughly half of all American silver. The money that funded Spain's American empire ended up in Hangzhou and Guangzhou.

For Spain, the silver was both windfall and trap. The sudden increase in money supply caused massive inflation — the "Price Revolution" we encountered in the money tutorial. It made Spanish manufactured goods uncompetitive. It funded wars that Spain ultimately couldn't win. Spain defaulted on its debts four times in the 16th century, despite receiving the largest infusion of precious metal in history. Being the source of the money is not the same as being good at using money.

Dutch Disease
The economic phenomenon where a large resource windfall (silver, oil, natural gas) causes a currency to appreciate, making other exports uncompetitive, hollowing out the manufacturing sector. Spain's silver is the original example. Norway's oil management — saving windfall revenues in a sovereign wealth fund rather than spending them — is the modern counter-example of how to handle resource wealth without being destroyed by it.

Gunpowder rewrites power

Gunpowder arrived in Europe from China via the Islamic world in the 13th century. It took another two centuries for European metallurgy and engineering to develop effective guns. By 1500, cannon had made the medieval stone castle obsolete — walls that had been impregnable for centuries could be reduced to rubble in days by artillery. The capital investment of the aristocracy — their castles, their armoured knights — was suddenly worthless.

This had enormous political consequences. A monarch with enough money to buy cannon could now destroy any noble who challenged them. The centralised nation-state — France under Louis XIV, England under the Tudors, Spain under Ferdinand and Isabella — was partly a product of gunpowder. Cannon equalised the military playing field in favour of whoever could afford the most guns, which meant in favour of centralised states with large tax bases over dispersed feudal lords with small ones.

In the Americas, the consequences were even more dramatic. The Spanish conquest of the Aztec Empire by Hernán Cortés in 1519–1521, with a force of roughly 600 men, was possible because of guns, steel armour, and horses — none of which existed in the Americas. But disease had already killed perhaps half the Aztec population by the time Cortés arrived. The conquest was a collision of technological and biological inequalities simultaneously.

Printing, the Reformation, and the first information war

Martin Luther's 95 Theses were printed in 1517. Within two weeks, copies had spread across Germany. Within a month, all of Europe. The printing press turned a local theological dispute into a continental crisis. This was not an accident — Luther understood perfectly what printing meant and used it deliberately. He wrote in German as well as Latin, explicitly targeting literate laypeople rather than just scholars.

The Catholic Church responded with its own printing campaigns. The result was the first large-scale information war: competing institutions mass-producing competing messages, fighting for the hearts and minds of a literate public. The techniques developed in this conflict — propaganda, counter-propaganda, appeals to vernacular emotion over Latin authority, the strategic leak of embarrassing information — are recognisable today.

The political consequences of the Reformation lasted a century and a half of warfare, ending only with the Peace of Westphalia in 1648 — the treaty that effectively established the modern nation-state system, with each state sovereign over its own territory and religion. The printing press didn't cause all of this, but it made the Reformation fast enough and widespread enough that it couldn't be suppressed as previous heresies had been.

Double-entry bookkeeping: the invisible technology

Double-entry bookkeeping was formalised by the Franciscan friar Luca Pacioli in 1494, though the practice was already in use in Italian merchant banking. The principle is simple: every transaction is recorded twice — as a debit in one account and a credit in another. The books must balance. If they don't, there's an error or fraud somewhere.

This sounds dry. Its consequences were enormous. For the first time, it was possible to have a clear, auditable picture of a complex enterprise's financial state at any moment. Investors could evaluate whether a business was profitable. Creditors could assess whether a borrower was creditworthy. Partners could detect fraud. The entire apparatus of modern finance — stock markets, corporate accounting, banking regulation — rests on this 15th-century innovation.

The philosopher Werner Sombart argued that double-entry bookkeeping was the foundation of capitalism — not just a tool for capitalism, but its intellectual foundation. The act of recording every transaction forces you to think about profit and loss as abstract quantities, separable from the physical goods being traded. It creates the concept of capital as something distinct from the things capital can buy. Whether Sombart was right in that strong claim, the practical point stands: without reliable accounting, large-scale enterprise is impossible, and without large-scale enterprise, the Industrial Revolution couldn't have been financed.

The joint-stock company: sharing risk to take more of it

The Dutch East India Company, founded in 1602, was the first modern joint-stock company — a legal entity that could own property, enter contracts, and sue and be sued independently of its shareholders. Its shares were traded on the Amsterdam Stock Exchange, the world's first. Shareholders could invest in the company, share in its profits, and sell their shares to someone else without the company itself having to do anything.

This was a revolutionary technology for managing risk. A single trading voyage to the Indies might take two or three years and could end in shipwreck, piracy, or simple commercial failure. No individual merchant could afford to bear that risk alone. By pooling risk across many investors, the joint-stock structure made voyages possible that would otherwise be unfinanceable. It also made the shareholders' liability limited — if the venture failed, you lost your investment, not everything you owned.

Real example
The Dutch East India Company as a state
Amsterdam · 1602–1799

The VOC — Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie — was not just a company. At its peak, it had 50,000 employees, 40 warships, and 150 trading ships. It had the power to wage war, negotiate treaties, mint its own coins, and establish colonies. It governed parts of what is now Indonesia for nearly 200 years.

The VOC was, in effect, a private state — a for-profit entity exercising sovereign power over foreign territories. This arrangement — private capital backed by state power, extracting resources from colonised peoples — became the template for European empire. The British East India Company followed the same model and eventually governed India. The consequences of this public-private hybrid of power are still being worked out today.

The Scientific Revolution

Between roughly 1543 (Copernicus's heliocentric model) and 1687 (Newton's Principia Mathematica), European natural philosophy transformed into something recognisably modern science. The key innovations were not discoveries but methods: controlled experiment, mathematical description of physical laws, the peer review of results through publication, and the separation of natural explanation from supernatural authority.

The Scientific Revolution didn't immediately improve technology in most fields — the gap between scientific understanding and practical application was long. Newton's laws of motion didn't design the steam engine; the steam engine's inventors were craftsmen working empirically. But the Scientific Revolution established an institutional infrastructure — universities, scientific societies, journals — and a culture of systematic inquiry that would, with a 150-year lag, feed directly into the Industrial Revolution.

It also created a new class of people: professional scientists and engineers, whose social role was to produce reliable knowledge about the natural world. Before this, knowledge production was the province of priests, philosophers, and artisans. The professionalisation of knowledge — the idea that you could have a career devoted to understanding how things work — was new and consequential.

Greece under the Ottomans

The Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453 extended over the following decades to cover most of Greece. By the early 16th century, the Greek-speaking world was largely under Ottoman rule. This is relevant to the technology story for two reasons.

First, Greek scholars fleeing the Ottoman advance carried classical manuscripts to Italy, directly fertilising the Renaissance. The Platonic Academy in Florence was founded by Cosimo de' Medici under the influence of the Byzantine philosopher Gemistus Pletho, who arrived in Florence for a church council in 1438 and stayed long enough to transform Florentine intellectual life. The Renaissance is partly a story of Greek knowledge coming home to the west via Byzantine refugees.

Second, Ottoman Greece sat at the intersection of European and Middle Eastern trade routes. Greek merchants and sailors were essential intermediaries in the eastern Mediterranean trade — multilingual, with contacts across the Ottoman and Venetian worlds, positioned perfectly to benefit from the commerce of the era. The Greek diaspora communities in Venice, Trieste, Amsterdam, and later London became significant economic actors precisely because their position gave them access to multiple markets and legal systems simultaneously.

Why not China, one more time

By 1600, China under the Ming dynasty had retreated from the technological frontier. The treasure ship voyages had stopped a century earlier. The printing press equivalent (movable type) wasn't producing the same explosion of public discourse. The Scientific Revolution was not happening there.

The historian Kenneth Pomeranz, in The Great Divergence, argues that China and western Europe were at broadly similar economic levels as late as 1750, and that the divergence came specifically from the combination of cheap coal in Britain (near the surface, near rivers for transport) and cheap raw materials from the Americas. China had coal but it was in the north, far from population centres. Without cheap energy and cheap raw materials, there was no economic incentive to develop labour-saving machinery at scale.

This is not a story about European superiority or Chinese failure. It's a story about geography, energy, and the specific contingencies that made one path more incentivised than others. Had Potosí been in China, or had England's coal seams been in Shanxi instead of Yorkshire, the story might have been different.

Standing on the edge of the machine

By 1750, the pieces of the Industrial Revolution were in place. Coal was being mined in enormous quantities in England — primarily to heat homes, since the forests had been largely depleted. Iron production was scaling up. A growing class of merchants and manufacturers, protected by increasingly reliable property rights, had both capital to invest and incentive to invest it in productivity improvements. The scientific culture of the Royal Society was beginning to connect with the practical culture of craftsmen and engineers.

The one missing piece was a way to turn heat into mechanical work at scale. Water mills and windmills had shown that natural forces could be harnessed. But water mills required a river and windmills required wind. What if you could make your own motive force, anywhere, from the coal already being dug out of the ground?

Thomas Newcomen built the first practical steam engine in 1712. It was inefficient, enormous, and useful only for pumping water out of mines. James Watt improved it in the 1760s by adding a separate condenser, dramatically reducing coal consumption. Matthew Boulton, Watt's business partner, understood the commercial potential and drove the commercialisation that turned an engineering invention into an industrial revolution.

The machine age was about to begin. And it would change everything — the city, the working day, the family, the empire, and the very texture of ordinary life — faster than anything that had come before.

"The Industrial Revolution was not a revolution in science. It was a revolution in the application of science — and the conditions for that application had been three centuries in the making."
