Tech,
rewinding the tape.

The history of technology, read through three lenses: what each invention did to money, what it did to power, and what it did to the texture of ordinary days.

Eight chapters · In progress · Built for the curious

Most histories of technology are lists — this invention, then that one, ever upward. This one tries to be something else: a causal story. Each chapter takes a stretch of time and asks the same three questions of whatever got built in it.

What did it do to money — to how value was stored, moved, and captured? What did it do to power — to who could coerce, persuade, or escape whom? And what did it do to daily life — to the ordinary texture of waking up, working, eating, and dying? Read that way, a stone blade and a language model turn out to rhyme.

The chapters are being drafted in order. The shells are here so the skeleton is visible; the flesh follows.

Why this exists

I kept reaching for technology metaphors to explain other things — money, institutions, why the present feels the way it does — and realised I didn't actually have the history straight. So I'm writing it down, the long way, to fix that. This guide is the result, still in progress.
