Chapter 06 · 1970 → 2010

The Digital Era

The microchip escaped the military, landed in garages, spawned an industry, built a network, and ate every other industry it touched — in forty years.

~44 min read 13 sections 1970 → 2010 AD

The chip that changed everything

In 1958, Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments and Robert Noyce at Fairchild Semiconductor independently invented the integrated circuit — a way to put multiple transistors on a single piece of semiconductor material. The first chips contained a handful of components. By 1971, Intel's 4004 microprocessor put 2,300 transistors on a chip the size of a fingernail. By 2024, a modern processor contains over 100 billion transistors on a chip roughly the same size.

This exponential growth was described by Gordon Moore in 1965 in what became known as Moore's Law: the number of transistors on a chip doubles approximately every two years, while the cost per transistor falls by half. Moore's Law held, with remarkable consistency, for about fifty years. It is not a law of nature — it's an observation about the pace of engineering progress, sustained by enormous investment and competitive pressure. But its effects were the defining economic fact of the late 20th century.

What Moore's Law means in practice is that computing power became, for fifty years, the fastest-falling cost in the history of commerce. A calculation that cost $1 to perform in 1970 cost less than one millionth of a cent by 2010. Nothing has ever become cheap that fast. This falling cost is the engine behind every digital product and service that now exists — the economics only work because the underlying computation is essentially free.

Moore's Law
Gordon Moore's 1965 observation that the number of transistors on an integrated circuit doubles roughly every two years. More loosely used to describe the consistent halving of computing costs over the same period. Moore's Law began slowing around 2015 as physical limits on transistor miniaturisation were approached. The industry has responded with new architectural approaches — multiple cores, specialised chips (GPUs, TPUs), 3D chip stacking — but the easy exponential gains of the classical era are over.

Why Silicon Valley

The concentration of the technology industry in a 50-kilometre strip of California between San Francisco and San Jose — Silicon Valley — is one of the most striking geographic concentrations of economic activity in history. Why there? Why not Boston, which had excellent universities and a strong electronics industry? Why not Tokyo or London?

The answer involves a specific combination of factors that came together in one place at one time and proved extraordinarily difficult to replicate. Stanford University, under the entrepreneurially minded dean Frederick Terman, actively encouraged its graduates to start companies nearby rather than taking jobs on the East Coast. The Cold War created enormous defence spending — the Federal government funded semiconductor research through military contracts, making the San Francisco Bay Area a centre of cutting-edge electronics work. Fairchild Semiconductor, founded in 1957 by eight researchers who left Texas Instruments, became the founding institution: the "Fairchildren" — companies founded by Fairchild alumni — include Intel, AMD, and dozens of others.

California's relatively weak non-compete law — compared to Massachusetts and most other states — meant that employees could leave one company and start or join a competitor without legal penalty. This made talent mobile and ideas spread fast. The culture of the 1960s counterculture, concentrated in the Bay Area, infused the early tech community with an idealism — the computer as liberation technology, democratising access to information and power — that shaped the products being built and the stories told about them.

The 360° context
Silicon Valley in 1975
California · 1975

Geopolitical: Cold War. The US military was the most important customer for advanced electronics, funding development through contracts that private markets couldn't have sustained. The Space Race had created a culture of ambitious engineering and enormous federal investment in technology.

Economic: The Vietnam War had ended, the oil shock of 1973 had hurt the traditional economy, inflation was high. But semiconductor companies were booming — the first commercial uses of integrated circuits in calculators and early computers were creating real demand beyond the military.

Cultural: The counterculture had generated a specific vision of the personal computer as a tool of individual liberation — an antidote to the faceless corporations and governments that IBM mainframes represented. Stewart Brand's Whole Earth Catalog mixed environmental consciousness, DIY ethos, and enthusiasm for technology in a way that directly influenced Steve Jobs and others.

Human need: The Homebrew Computer Club, which Jobs and Wozniak attended, was full of people who wanted to own and control their own computers — not rent time on a mainframe owned by a corporation or university. Personal ownership of computing power was the driving desire.

The personal computer

In January 1975, the Altair 8800 appeared on the cover of Popular Electronics. It was a kit computer — you assembled it yourself, it had no keyboard or screen, and you programmed it by flipping switches on the front panel. It was technically useless for almost everything. It sold out immediately.

The Altair 8800 was the spark. Two young men in Albuquerque — Bill Gates and Paul Allen — saw the cover and immediately wrote a version of the BASIC programming language for it, licensing it to the manufacturer. A year later, they moved to Seattle and founded Microsoft. Meanwhile in California, Steve Wozniak built a computer he called Apple I and showed it at the Homebrew Computer Club. Steve Jobs saw the commercial potential, and Apple Computer was founded in April 1976.

IBM entered the market in 1981 with the IBM PC, and made two decisions that would define the industry for decades. First, it built the PC from commodity components — an Intel processor, a Microsoft operating system — rather than proprietary IBM hardware. Second, it published the technical specifications, enabling a market in IBM-compatible clones. Within five years, the IBM-compatible PC standard had become the industry standard, but IBM itself had given away the valuable pieces: Intel controlled the processor and Microsoft controlled the operating system. Both became the most profitable companies in the technology industry. IBM, which had invented the standard, was displaced.

Microsoft and the software empire

The fundamental insight that made Microsoft was Gates's: in a world where hardware was commoditising rapidly (falling in price as chips got cheaper), software was the bottleneck. The operating system — the software that managed the hardware and on which all other software ran — was the strategic chokepoint. Control the operating system and you controlled the platform.

MS-DOS, and then Windows, achieved something rare in technology: a genuine network monopoly. The more people used Windows, the more software developers wrote for Windows, the more people bought Windows, the more people bought Windows-compatible hardware. This self-reinforcing cycle — economists call it a network effect — made Windows nearly impossible to displace, not because it was the best operating system (it wasn't) but because it was the standard. Like the QWERTY keyboard layout or the standard railway gauge, Windows was valuable primarily because everyone else used it.

By the mid-1990s, Microsoft was the most valuable company in the world. Gates was the richest person alive. And the US Department of Justice was building a case that Microsoft had used its operating system monopoly to illegally crush competitors — including Netscape, whose browser had threatened to make the operating system irrelevant by moving computing into the web. The antitrust case, settled in 2001, was inconclusive in legal terms but historically important: it occupied Microsoft's management during the years when Google, Apple's iPod, and the open-source movement all emerged to challenge its dominance.

The internet's accidental origins

ARPANET — the Advanced Research Projects Agency Network — first transmitted data between computers at UCLA and Stanford Research Institute on 29 October 1969. The message was supposed to be "LOGIN." The system crashed after the first two letters. The first message transmitted on the internet was "LO."

ARPANET was built to solve a specific Cold War problem: how do you maintain military communications if Soviet nuclear weapons destroy major command centres? The answer was a distributed network with no central node — packets of data could route around damage, finding alternative paths to their destination. This design philosophy — decentralisation, redundancy, no single point of failure — was baked into the internet's architecture from its military origins. It later made the internet extraordinarily difficult to censor or control, which was not what the military had in mind.

The key technical innovation was packet switching: rather than dedicating a fixed circuit between two points (as telephone networks did), data was broken into packets, each labelled with its destination, and routed independently through whatever network path was available. The packets were reassembled at the destination. This made the network enormously more efficient and resilient than circuit switching. Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn developed TCP/IP — the protocol suite that defines how data moves across the internet — in 1974. It is still what the internet runs on today.

The web: the internet made visible

The internet existed for twenty years before most people knew about it. In 1989, it was used primarily by universities, government researchers, and defence contractors for email and file transfer. Then, in March 1989, a British physicist at CERN named Tim Berners-Lee submitted a proposal to his manager for a system of linked documents accessible over the internet. His manager wrote in the margin: "Vague but exciting."

Berners-Lee's World Wide Web — built on hypertext (clickable links between documents), URLs (addresses for every document), and HTTP (the protocol for requesting and delivering them) — went live in 1991. In 1993, Marc Andreessen and colleagues at the University of Illinois released Mosaic, the first graphical web browser. Suddenly the internet had pictures. You could click links. You could navigate information without knowing anything about the underlying technology. The web was the internet made accessible to non-engineers, and its adoption was explosive.

The web democratised publishing in a way that printing had for the written word 500 years earlier. Before the web, creating and distributing content required either owning a press, a broadcast licence, or access to a distribution network. The web gave everyone a global publishing platform for the cost of a hosting account. The early web was genuinely chaotic and genuinely open — anyone could publish anything, and the architecture gave no single entity control over what appeared.

The dotcom boom and bust

Between 1995 and 2000, investors poured approximately $5 trillion into internet companies, most of which had no profits, many of which had no revenue, and some of which had no product beyond a PowerPoint deck and a domain name. The NASDAQ index rose 400% between 1995 and March 2000. It then fell 78% over the following two years. Hundreds of companies went bankrupt. Trillions of dollars of paper wealth evaporated.

The dotcom bubble followed the template of every previous technology bubble — the tulip mania, the railway mania, the radio stocks of the 1920s — with one important difference: some of what was being speculated on turned out to be real. Amazon survived the crash and became one of the most valuable companies in history. Google, which went public in 2004, was the most valuable IPO of its era. The infrastructure — the fibre-optic cables, the server farms, the protocols — laid down during the boom remained in place and became the backbone of the subsequent internet economy.

The pattern of infrastructure bubbles

Technology bubbles often leave useful infrastructure behind. The railway mania of the 1840s bankrupted thousands of investors but covered Britain in railways that served the economy for 150 years. The dotcom bubble bankrupted thousands of investors but covered the world in fibre-optic cable that supported the 2010s internet economy. The speculative excess funds real construction. This doesn't justify the fraud and misallocation — but it does mean that "the bubble burst" doesn't mean "nothing real was built."

Google and the attention economy

Larry Page and Sergey Brin founded Google in 1998. Their innovation was PageRank: rather than ranking web pages by how many times they contained a search term (as earlier search engines did), PageRank ranked them by how many other pages linked to them. A page linked to by many high-quality pages was more likely to be authoritative than one linked to by few. The results were dramatically better than the competition, and Google grew from nothing to the dominant search engine in roughly three years.

Google's business model — which took several years to emerge — was not search. It was advertising. Specifically, the sale of attention: a user performing a search had demonstrated, by the search itself, an interest in something. An advertiser could pay to appear next to results for that search and reach users at the precise moment of relevant interest. This was more valuable than any previous advertising medium, because it was targeted, measurable, and tied to demonstrated intent rather than assumed demographics.

The attention economy — the business model in which a product or service is offered free to users, while users' attention is sold to advertisers — became the dominant model of the consumer internet. Google, Facebook, Twitter, YouTube: all are attention businesses. The product is the user's time and attention; the customer is the advertiser. This model has produced enormous wealth, funded extraordinary products and services, and created structural incentives to maximise engagement at the expense of almost everything else. The addiction dynamics of social media, the spread of outrage and misinformation, the difficulty of finding reliable information amid a sea of attention-optimised content — all are downstream of this economic structure.

The phone becomes a computer

On 9 January 2007, Steve Jobs stood on a stage in San Francisco and announced "three revolutionary products": a widescreen iPod, a revolutionary mobile phone, and a breakthrough internet communications device. Then he revealed they were all the same thing: the iPhone.

The iPhone was not the first smartphone. BlackBerry had been making phones with email and rudimentary web browsing for years. What the iPhone did was put a touchscreen, a fast processor, a good camera, and a genuinely usable mobile web browser into a single device and make it feel obvious and inevitable. The App Store, launched a year later, opened the platform to third-party developers and created an entirely new software market.

The smartphone is arguably the most consequential consumer technology product ever made. By 2010, there were 5 billion mobile phone subscriptions globally; by 2020, over 6 billion people owned smartphones — more than had access to running water. The smartphone put a computer, a camera, a map, a library, a bank, a social network, and a communication device into the pocket of most of humanity. It democratised access to information and services in ways that no previous technology had achieved at this speed and scale.

It also put a tracking device, an attention capture mechanism, and a social comparison engine into those same pockets. The smartphone is not a neutral tool. Its design embeds choices — about what's easy, what's prominent, what's addictive — made by engineers in California optimising for engagement metrics.

Social media and the rewired public sphere

Facebook launched in 2004, Twitter in 2006, YouTube in 2005, Instagram in 2010. By 2010, social media had become the dominant mode of communication for hundreds of millions of people, and the primary news source for many of them. The public sphere — the space where societies argue about shared concerns — had migrated from newspapers, television, and physical public space to platforms owned by private companies optimising for engagement.

The Arab Spring of 2010–12, in which mass protests toppled authoritarian governments across the Arab world, was widely attributed to social media's ability to organise protests and spread information faster than governments could suppress it. The optimistic narrative was powerful: social media as liberation technology, enabling the oppressed to coordinate against the powerful.

The counter-evidence accumulated quickly. The same platforms that enabled Arab Spring protesters enabled ISIS recruiters, anti-vaccine propagandists, and authoritarian governments learning to use social media to monitor and manipulate their own citizens. The engagement-maximising algorithms that made platforms profitable also maximised outrage, tribalism, and misinformation — because these generated more clicks, shares, and time-on-platform than calm, accurate information. By 2016, when social media had demonstrably influenced elections in multiple countries, the liberation narrative was hard to sustain.

The platform economy

Amazon, Google, Facebook, Apple, and Microsoft — the five most valuable companies in the world by 2020 — are all platforms. Not manufacturers, not service providers in the traditional sense, but platforms: infrastructure on which other businesses and individuals operate, with the platform owner extracting a toll from every transaction.

The platform model achieves something that previous monopolies couldn't: it scales without proportional cost increases. Adding another seller to Amazon's marketplace costs Amazon almost nothing. Adding another developer to the App Store costs Apple almost nothing. Adding another advertiser to Google's system costs Google almost nothing. The marginal cost of scale approaches zero, while the network effects that make the platform valuable increase with every additional user. This is the economic structure of monopoly in its purest form — winner-takes-most markets with no obvious ceiling.

By 2010, the concentration of the internet's economic value in a small number of American platforms had become visible and troubling. European regulators — the EU's competition authorities in particular — were beginning to develop frameworks for regulating platform power. The US, where the platforms were headquartered and where the political culture was less comfortable with industrial policy, was slower to respond. The debate about platform power — how much is too much, how should it be regulated, who benefits and who is harmed — was the defining technology policy question of the early 21st century.

Greece enters the digital age

Greece's digital transition in the 1990s and 2000s was shaped by two factors that distinguished it from northern European countries: late and uneven infrastructure development, and the economic crisis that began in 2010 and dramatically changed the context for digital adoption.

Broadband internet arrived later in Greece than in most of western Europe, and mobile network coverage outside major cities remained patchy into the 2000s. The digital economy was concentrated in Athens and Thessaloniki. Small businesses and the agricultural sector — still a significant part of the Greek economy — were slow to digitise.

The financial crisis that began in 2010 had a paradoxical effect on digital adoption. On one hand, it devastated the economy and reduced investment in infrastructure. On the other, it created powerful incentives to use digital platforms for commerce, communication, and service delivery — because digital was cheap, and cheap mattered desperately. Greek entrepreneurs built successful software companies, digital agencies, and technology startups despite the economic environment. The crisis also accelerated the adoption of digital government services, as the conditions attached to international bailouts required modernisation of tax collection and public administration.

By 2010, Greek internet penetration was around 44% — below the EU average but growing rapidly. The smartphone arrived in Greece at the same time as everywhere else; mobile-first digital adoption, skipping the PC-era patterns of northern Europe, became common. The same pattern — mobile-first leapfrogging — was happening across the developing world simultaneously.

What actually changed

The digital era changed three things that matter more than the specific technologies that enabled them.

First, it made the marginal cost of copying and distributing information essentially zero. Before digital, making a copy of anything — a book, a song, a film, a design — required physical material and physical labour. After digital, making a million copies cost the same as making one. This destroyed the economics of every industry built on scarcity of information: music, news, film, encyclopaedias, maps, classified advertising. It created enormous consumer surplus — most of human knowledge, freely accessible — while devastating the industries that had profited from information scarcity.

Second, it made location irrelevant for an increasing range of economic activities. A programmer in Bucharest could work for a company in San Francisco. A customer service representative in Manila could support customers in London. An entrepreneur in Athens could sell products globally from a laptop. This was genuinely new. For all of human history before the internet, economic geography was physical geography. Now it partly wasn't.

Third, it shifted attention — the scarce resource in an information-abundant world — to the centre of the economy. The companies that captured and retained human attention captured the economic value of the digital era. This was not a neutral technical fact. It embedded a specific set of values — engagement over accuracy, novelty over depth, emotional provocation over considered thought — into the infrastructure of the public sphere. We are still working out the consequences.

"The internet is the world's largest library. It's just that all the books are on the floor." — often attributed to John Allen Paulos

By 2010, a new set of capabilities was beginning to emerge that would once again shift the landscape. Machine learning had been quietly developing for decades; deep learning, enabled by GPU computing power and enormous datasets, was beginning to produce results that seemed impossible: computers that could recognise faces, translate languages, play Go at superhuman level. The AI era was beginning. That's the next chapter.
