Chapter 05 · 1900 → 1970

The Electric & Atomic Century

Light, flight, the bomb, and the broadcast — seventy years that wired the world, killed a hundred million people, and accidentally invented the machine that would change everything else.

~45 min read 13 sections 1900 → 1970 AD

Electricity goes everywhere

In 1900, electrification was an urban novelty enjoyed by a wealthy minority. By 1930, it had transformed daily life across the developed world. The speed of this transformation — thirty years from curiosity to infrastructure — is one of the fastest technology adoptions in history, and it happened because electricity is uniquely versatile.

Steam power was powerful but local. A steam engine had to be next to the thing it powered — the mill, the pump, the locomotive. Electricity could be generated anywhere and transmitted anywhere. A single large generating station could power a thousand factories, a million homes, a city's entire lighting system. The factory floor, which had previously been organised around a single central steam engine with belts and shafts running to every machine, could now give each machine its own electric motor. This allowed factories to be redesigned from scratch, with machines arranged for workflow rather than proximity to a power shaft. Productivity jumped.

The second industrial revolution

Economists distinguish two industrial revolutions: the first (steam, textiles, iron, 1760–1840) and the second (electricity, chemicals, steel, internal combustion, 1870–1914). The second was arguably more consequential for daily life. The first gave us factories and railways. The second gave us electric light, the telephone, the automobile, synthetic fertilisers, and aspirin. It transformed the home as profoundly as the factory.

Tesla, Edison, and the war of currents

Thomas Edison built the first commercial electricity system in New York in 1882, using direct current (DC). It worked, but DC had a fundamental limitation: voltage drops over distance, meaning a DC power station could only serve customers within about 1.5 kilometres. You needed a generating station in every neighbourhood.

Nikola Tesla, working initially for Edison and then for his rival George Westinghouse, developed an alternating current (AC) system that could step voltage up for long-distance transmission and back down for safe household use. AC could transmit power over hundreds of kilometres from a single large generating station. It was clearly superior for grid-scale electricity distribution.

Edison, having invested heavily in DC infrastructure, fought back — famously and unscrupulously. He publicly electrocuted animals with AC current to demonstrate its danger, and lobbied for the electric chair to use AC, hoping to associate Tesla's system with execution. None of it worked. The Niagara Falls power station, built on AC technology in 1895, could power all of Buffalo, New York from 35 kilometres away. The economics were decisive. AC won, and the electrical grid we use today runs on Tesla's principles.

Real example
The war of currents as a business story
USA · 1880–1895

The Tesla-Edison conflict is often told as a story of genius versus stubbornness. It's more accurately a story of sunk cost, incumbency, and the difficulty of admitting a technical choice was wrong. Edison had built a business around DC. Switching to AC would have required writing off that investment and starting over. His resistance wasn't irrational — it was the rational response of someone protecting existing capital against a disruptive technology. This pattern — incumbents fighting technically superior alternatives because switching costs are high — repeats throughout the history of technology. The incumbent usually loses eventually. The question is how long it takes and how much damage the delay causes.

Light and the end of night

For all of human history before the late 19th century, night was genuinely dark. Candles and oil lamps provided light, but expensive, dim, and flickering light — not enough to read comfortably or work productively for extended periods. When the sun went down, most people went to bed.

Edison's incandescent light bulb, commercially viable from 1879, and the electrification that followed over the next thirty years, ended this. Artificial light became cheap enough to use casually. The working day could extend into the evening. Shops and theatres and restaurants could operate after dark. Cities became genuinely nocturnal in a way that was impossible before electricity.

The social consequences were large and underappreciated. The extension of the working day into evening hours was partly beneficial — more time for leisure, culture, socialising — and partly exploitative: factories could now run night shifts, extending the working day in ways that pure human endurance had previously capped. Electricity was, from the beginning, both liberation and new form of control, depending on who you were.

Flight: the 12-second revolution

On 17 December 1903, at Kitty Hawk, North Carolina, Orville Wright flew a powered heavier-than-air machine for 12 seconds. The Wright brothers made four flights that day; the longest lasted 59 seconds and covered 260 metres. Within six years, Louis Blériot had crossed the English Channel. Within eleven years, aircraft were killing people by the thousands on the Western Front. Within thirty years, commercial aviation was established across the Atlantic. Within sixty-six years, a human being stood on the moon.

The speed of aviation's development from first flight to moon landing is staggering: sixty-six years, less than a human lifetime. It was driven partly by the extraordinary demands of two world wars, which poured resources into aviation development at a rate that peacetime commerce could never have sustained, and partly by the basic physical fact that flight, once achieved, was obviously useful for everything from mail delivery to bombing cities to crossing oceans.

Flight compressed geography. A world in which crossing the Atlantic took two weeks became a world in which it took seven hours. This compression made global business, global tourism, and global warfare all dramatically more feasible. The Boeing 747, which entered service in 1970 and could carry 400 passengers, made mass international travel affordable for the first time — democratising a form of mobility that had previously been reserved for the wealthy.

Radio and the mass mind

Guglielmo Marconi demonstrated wireless telegraphy in 1895 and crossed the Atlantic with a radio signal in 1901. By the 1920s, commercial radio broadcasting had begun in the United States and Britain. By the 1930s, it was the dominant mass medium in the industrialised world — reaching into homes, farms, and factories with news, music, drama, and, fatally, propaganda.

Radio was the first broadcast technology — one sender, many receivers, simultaneously. Before radio, information spread through newspapers (which required literacy), word of mouth (which was slow and local), and public meetings (which required physical presence). Radio could reach everyone who owned a receiver, instantly, regardless of literacy or location.

The political implications were immediately understood and immediately exploited. Franklin Roosevelt's "fireside chats" — radio addresses that created the illusion of an intimate conversation between the president and each individual American family — were a masterclass in using the medium's intimate quality to build political connection at scale. Adolf Hitler's use of radio was more direct: the Nazi regime manufactured cheap "People's Receivers" (the Volksempfänger) specifically to maximise radio penetration in Germany. By 1939, Germany had the highest radio ownership rate in the world. The same technology that let Roosevelt reassure a nation through the Depression let Goebbels coordinate one of history's most effective propaganda campaigns.

Broadcast vs. network
Radio and television are broadcast technologies: one-to-many, with no feedback mechanism. The audience receives but cannot respond. The internet is a network technology: many-to-many, with continuous feedback. This structural difference has profound political consequences. Broadcast media tend to centralise cultural and political power — a small number of senders shape the worldview of a large number of receivers. Network media tend to fragment it, enabling any receiver to become a sender. Whether fragmentation is better than centralisation depends heavily on what people say when they can say anything.

The first industrial war

The First World War (1914–1918) was the first conflict in which industrial technology was deployed at full scale on both sides simultaneously. The result was catastrophe: approximately 17 million dead, the destruction of four empires, and a degree of industrial-scale killing that traumatised European civilisation for a generation.

The technologies that defined the war were mostly 19th-century inventions meeting 19th-century tactical thinking with catastrophic results. The machine gun could fire 400–600 rounds per minute — enough to cut down an advancing infantry line in seconds. The artillery of 1914–18 was vastly more powerful and accurate than anything seen before; the Battle of the Somme began with a seven-day artillery bombardment that fired 1.5 million shells. Poison gas — chlorine and mustard gas — was deployed for the first time, causing 1.3 million casualties.

The tactical problem was that offensive technology had outrun defensive technology. You could advance across no-man's-land under artillery cover, but holding gained ground against a counter-attack was nearly impossible. The result was four years of trench warfare — millions of men living in muddy holes, dying in mass advances that gained metres at a cost of thousands of lives.

The war also produced the first military aircraft, the first tanks, and the first large-scale use of motor vehicles for logistics. It industrialised medicine — field surgery, blood transfusion, and triage at scale — and industrial chemistry for explosives and gases. War remained, as it had been since the Bronze Age, the most powerful driver of technological development per unit of time.

Feeding the world, arming the world

Fritz Haber was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1918 for a process that arguably saved more lives than any other single invention in history — and directly enabled the industrial killing of the First World War.

The Haber-Bosch process, developed between 1909 and 1913, synthesises ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen. Ammonia is the basis of synthetic fertiliser. Before Haber-Bosch, the world's agriculture depended on fixed nitrogen from guano (seabird droppings, mined from Pacific islands), Chilean saltpetre, and the slow process of crop rotation with nitrogen-fixing plants. The supply was limited. The human population was constrained by how much food these nitrogen sources could produce.

Haber-Bosch broke that constraint. Synthetic fertiliser, derived from atmospheric nitrogen, could be produced in unlimited quantities. World food production expanded dramatically. The global population, which was 1.6 billion in 1900, is now 8 billion — a fivefold increase in a century. Demographers estimate that roughly half the nitrogen in the bodies of today's humans came from the Haber-Bosch process. Without it, the Earth could probably support 3–4 billion people at current agricultural methods. The other 4–5 billion of us exist because of a chemistry patent from 1913.

The same chemistry produced the explosives that killed 17 million people in the First World War. The nitrogen fixed for fertiliser and the nitrogen fixed for TNT and artillery shells are chemically related processes. Haber personally supervised the first use of chlorine gas as a weapon at Ypres in 1915. His wife, also a chemist, shot herself with his service pistol in protest shortly after. He continued.

The automobile redraws the city

Henry Ford's moving assembly line, introduced in 1913 at Highland Park, Michigan, reduced the time to build a Model T from 12.5 hours to 93 minutes. The price fell from $850 in 1908 to $260 in 1924. By 1927, Ford had sold 15 million Model Ts. The automobile went from rich man's toy to working-class consumer good in twenty years.

The automobile did to the city what the railway did to the nation: it reshaped the geography of daily life around a new transport technology. Cities built for pedestrians and trams were redesigned for cars. Suburbs — low-density residential areas accessible only by car — became the dominant form of urban expansion in the United States and increasingly in Europe. The distance between home and work, home and shops, home and school expanded. The car became not a luxury but a prerequisite for participation in normal life.

The oil industry, which had existed since the 1860s primarily to supply kerosene for lighting, was transformed by the automobile into the strategic resource of the 20th century. Access to oil became a determinant of military and economic power. The First World War was the last major conflict fought primarily on coal; the Second World War was a war about oil as much as territory. Japan attacked Pearl Harbor partly to prevent the US from interfering with its seizure of Dutch East Indies oil. Germany's invasion of the Soviet Union aimed partly at the Caucasus oil fields. The Middle East became strategically central to global power precisely because it sat on the largest proven oil reserves.

The second war and its technologies

The Second World War (1939–1945) killed approximately 70–85 million people — the deadliest conflict in human history — and produced a technological acceleration without precedent. The urgency of war, combined with the industrial capacity of the major powers and the intellectual concentration of millions of engineers and scientists in military programmes, compressed decades of technological development into six years.

Radar — the ability to detect aircraft and ships at long range using reflected radio waves — was developed independently in Britain, Germany, and the United States in the late 1930s. Britain's early investment in a chain of radar stations along its southern coast was a decisive factor in the Battle of Britain in 1940, allowing RAF Fighter Command to direct limited fighter resources precisely to where attacking German formations were. Radar then became the foundation for modern air traffic control, weather forecasting, and eventually GPS.

Penicillin, discovered by Alexander Fleming in 1928 but not produced at scale until wartime need made it urgent, saved hundreds of thousands of lives from infected wounds. The pharmaceutical industry that could mass-produce antibiotics emerged from wartime investment and transformed medicine permanently. The average life expectancy in Britain in 1900 was 47 years; by 1970 it was 72. Antibiotics were the single largest contributor to that increase.

The V-2 rocket, developed by Wernher von Braun's team for Nazi Germany, was the world's first ballistic missile. It was militarily ineffective but technically extraordinary — the first human-made object to reach the edge of space. Von Braun surrendered to American forces in 1945, was brought to the United States under Operation Paperclip, and eventually directed the NASA programme that put humans on the moon. The Apollo Saturn V rocket was a direct descendant of the V-2. The space programme was built on Nazi engineering by a man who had used concentration camp labour to build his missiles and later pleaded ignorance of the conditions.

The atom split

On 16 July 1945, in the New Mexico desert, the United States detonated the first nuclear device. The physicist J. Robert Oppenheimer, watching the fireball, recalled a line from the Bhagavad Gita: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds." Three weeks later, atomic bombs destroyed Hiroshima and Nagasaki. The war ended. The nuclear age began.

The Manhattan Project that built the bomb was the largest scientific undertaking in history to that point — 130,000 people, $2 billion (roughly $30 billion in today's money), the concentrated effort of most of the world's best physicists, many of them European Jews who had fled Nazi Germany. The bomb that killed 200,000 people in two Japanese cities was also the product of Einstein's E=mc², Fermi's nuclear reactor, Bohr's model of the atom, and the entire edifice of 20th-century physics. Pure science and mass destruction arrived together, inseparably.

The Cold War that followed was structured around nuclear weapons. The United States and Soviet Union each built arsenals capable of destroying human civilisation many times over. This mutual assured destruction — MAD, with appropriate irony — created a strange stability: the two superpowers never fought each other directly, because the consequence of doing so was too obviously catastrophic. Instead they fought through proxies, in Korea, Vietnam, Angola, Afghanistan, and dozens of other places. The nuclear standoff froze the map of great-power conflict while producing extraordinary suffering in smaller states caught between the giants.

The 360° context
Why the bomb was built when it was
USA · 1939–1945

Scientific: The physics was ready. Fission had been demonstrated in December 1938 in Berlin. Einstein wrote to Roosevelt in August 1939 warning that Germany might be building a bomb. The theoretical framework for a weapon existed; what was needed was industrial-scale engineering.

Geopolitical: The fear that Nazi Germany would build a bomb first was the driving motivation. It turned out Germany's nuclear programme was far behind — partly because the Nazi regime had expelled most of its best physicists as Jews. The weapon was ready in July 1945, two months after Germany's surrender. It was used against Japan, the remaining enemy.

Economic: Only the United States had the industrial capacity and geographical safety to build the bomb. The Soviet Union, Britain, and Germany were fighting on their own soil. American factories were untouchable by enemy air power.

Human: The scientists who built it mostly believed, at the time, that they were preventing a worse outcome — a Nazi bomb. By 1945, with Germany defeated, many had serious doubts. The Franck Report, signed by several Manhattan Project scientists in June 1945, recommended a demonstration detonation rather than use on a populated city. It was ignored.

The computer is born in wartime

Alan Turing published his theoretical description of a universal computing machine in 1936 — a mathematical abstraction of a device that could perform any computable function if given the right program. It was pure mathematics, with no practical application in sight.

Six years later, Turing was working at Bletchley Park, the British codebreaking centre, designing electromechanical machines to break the German Enigma cipher. The Bombe — not quite a computer in the modern sense, but a programmable machine for testing cipher possibilities — was running by 1940. By 1943, Colossus — arguably the world's first programmable electronic computer — was operational at Bletchley, breaking the Lorenz cipher used by Hitler's high command.

Simultaneously in the United States, ENIAC — Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer — was being built at the University of Pennsylvania, completed in 1945 and initially used to calculate artillery firing tables. ENIAC weighed 30 tonnes, occupied 170 square metres, and contained 18,000 vacuum tubes that collectively failed every few days. It could perform about 5,000 additions per second. Your smartphone performs several billion.

The conceptual breakthrough was Turing's: a single machine that could do anything, if programmed appropriately. The physical breakthrough was the vacuum tube, and then the transistor (invented at Bell Labs in 1947), which replaced fragile, power-hungry vacuum tubes with small, robust, efficient switches. The transistor is the foundational component of all modern electronics. Without it, the digital world doesn't exist.

Greece, occupation, and reconstruction

Greece entered the Second World War on 28 October 1940, when Mussolini's Italy issued an ultimatum demanding passage for Italian troops. The Greek dictator Ioannis Metaxas reportedly replied with a single word: "No." — Óchi. Greece repelled the Italian invasion and counter-attacked into Albania, the first Allied victory on land in the war. Germany intervened in April 1941 and conquered Greece in three weeks.

The Axis occupation from 1941 to 1944 was devastating. The Great Famine of 1941–42 — caused by Axis requisitioning of food supplies combined with a British naval blockade — killed between 100,000 and 300,000 people in Athens and the surrounding region alone. Entire villages were massacred in reprisals for resistance activity. Jewish communities that had existed in Thessaloniki since antiquity — the Sephardic community, expelled from Spain in 1492 and settled in the Ottoman empire — were almost entirely deported to Auschwitz. Of approximately 60,000 Greek Jews, roughly 87% were killed.

After liberation in 1944, Greece immediately entered a civil war between communist and anti-communist forces that lasted until 1949. The country that emerged from a decade of war and occupation was devastated — its infrastructure destroyed, its economy collapsed, its society traumatised. The Marshall Plan and American military support helped rebuild, but Greece's 20th century was defined as much by these catastrophes as by any technological development.

What was built

Step back from the catastrophes of the first half of the 20th century and look at what was constructed. The electrical grid. Mass aviation. The automobile infrastructure. The pharmaceutical industry. Radio and television. Nuclear power. And, in its early, tentative form, the digital computer.

Between 1900 and 1970, the material conditions of life in the industrialised world changed more dramatically than in all previous human history. Life expectancy doubled. Infant mortality fell by 90%. The working week shortened from 70 hours to 40. The washing machine, the refrigerator, and central heating transformed the domestic sphere, largely freeing women from the most physically exhausting household labour (though not from the rest of it). Access to recorded music, film, and broadcast entertainment gave ordinary people forms of cultural experience that had previously been available only to the wealthy.

By 1970, a new technology was beginning its escape from the military and academic institutions where it had been born. The integrated circuit — the microchip — had been invented in 1958. The ARPANET, precursor of the internet, had first transmitted data in 1969. A generation of engineers and entrepreneurs, many of them formed by the counterculture of the 1960s and the scientific culture of the Cold War, were about to build the digital world. That story is the next chapter.

"The most important invention of the 20th century was not the atom bomb or the automobile or the television. It was the method of invention itself." — adapted from Alfred North Whitehead
