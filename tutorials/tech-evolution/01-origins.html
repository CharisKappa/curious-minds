Origins

From the first sharpened stone to the first city — the technologies that made us human, made surplus possible, and set every pattern that follows.

~40 min read 13 sections 3 million BC → 0 AD

The question nobody asks

Here's a question that sounds simple until you sit with it: why did the Industrial Revolution happen in 18th-century Britain, and not in ancient Rome? The Romans had steam. They had it in the form of Hero of Alexandria's aeolipile — a spinning ball powered by steam jets, built around 50 AD. They had cheap metal, skilled craftsmen, enormous demand for labour-saving machines in their mines and farms. And yet the steam engine didn't arrive for another 1,700 years.

Or take this one: China invented printing four centuries before Gutenberg. They had gunpowder, the compass, paper, cast iron. By any accounting, China in 1000 AD was the most technologically advanced civilisation on earth. And yet the Industrial Revolution didn't happen there either.

These aren't trivia questions. They're the whole point. Technology doesn't march forward on its own. It moves when conditions — economic, political, social, energetic — make it worth moving. Understanding those conditions is the only way to understand why we live in the world we do, and not some other one.

The frame for this whole guide

Every chapter asks three questions of whatever technology it covers: what did it do to money — to how value was created, stored, and captured? What did it do to power — to who could coerce, persuade, or escape whom? And what did it do to daily life — to the texture of ordinary waking hours? Read that way, a stone blade and a language model turn out to be telling the same story.

The first tool

The oldest known stone tools are about 3.3 million years old, found in Kenya in 2015. Their makers weren't even Homo sapiens — our species is only about 300,000 years old. Some earlier hominin, possibly Australopithecus, was already picking up rocks and knapping their edges into cutting surfaces.

What did this tool do to power? Immediately and obviously: it changed the predator-prey relationship. A creature with a sharp edge can process a carcass faster, access marrow other scavengers can't reach, and defend itself differently. The caloric returns from scavenging and hunting jumped. Bodies could afford to grow larger brains. Larger brains made better tools. The loop was open.

What it did to daily life is harder to see but just as profound. A stone tool is the externalisation of intent. Every animal uses its body — claws, teeth, strength — as its primary interface with the world. A tool user is different: it has a body plus an extension, and crucially, that extension can be put down, picked up by someone else, improved, passed on. Technology is, at its root, the capacity to store capability outside a single body and a single lifetime.

Technology
From Greek techne (craft, skill) + logos (knowledge). In this guide: any tool, method, or system that extends human capability beyond what a single unaided body can do. A stone blade counts. So does double-entry bookkeeping. So does a legal contract.

Fire: the original platform

Controlled use of fire appears in the fossil record around 400,000 years ago, possibly earlier. The anthropologist Richard Wrangham has argued — convincingly — that cooking fire is the single most consequential technology in human evolution. Here's why.

Raw food requires enormous digestive effort. Cooking pre-digests food, breaking down proteins and starches, releasing far more calories per mouthful. A cooked diet can sustain a large brain on less gut. The human digestive tract is proportionally much smaller than other great apes — evidence that we evolved alongside cooking, not before it. In a very real sense, fire didn't just change what we ate. It changed what we are.

But fire did something else, something that matters enormously for understanding technology: it created the campfire. A place of warmth, safety, and extended waking hours after dark. Language, story, and social bonding all accelerated around that fire. The first classroom, the first meeting room, the first theatre — all of them are the campfire. Every technology that enables people to gather, communicate, and collaborate at scale is, in some sense, a better campfire.

The 360° context
What the world looked like when fire was mastered
~400,000 BC

Geopolitical: No states, no empires. Small bands of 20–50 individuals, loosely connected to neighbouring bands by kinship and occasional trade. Territory was defended but not owned.

Energy: Entirely human and animal muscle, plus fire. The energy budget of a group was the food they could find or kill. Fire multiplied the caloric value of that food dramatically.

Economic: No money, no markets. Pure gift economy and reciprocal obligation within the band. Surplus was shared immediately — there was no way to store it.

Human needs: Safety from predators and rival bands, enough calories, warmth. Fire addressed all three at once.

The deepest pattern here: fire was not invented by a lone genius. It was developed, improved, and transmitted by communities over thousands of generations. Most transformative technologies work this way — they look like a single invention but are actually an accumulation of small improvements by many people across long periods of time.

Language: the invisible tool

We don't know when language appeared. It leaves no direct fossil trace. But the best current estimate puts complex language at somewhere between 100,000 and 50,000 years ago — coinciding with a dramatic acceleration in human behaviour that archaeologists call the Upper Palaeolithic Revolution. Suddenly, after millions of years of relatively static tool technology, humans began making art, wearing jewellery, burying their dead with ceremony, and trading objects hundreds of kilometres from their source.

Language is a technology in the deepest sense: it allows one mind to model another. When you use language, you're not just making sounds — you're constructing a representation of your internal state and transmitting it into another person's brain with enough precision that they can act on it. No other animal does this at human scale.

What language did to power was radical. Before language, knowledge died with its owner. After language, knowledge could be transmitted, accumulated, and refined across generations. A grandmother could tell her grandchildren about a drought that happened fifty years ago. A hunter could describe prey behaviour to someone who'd never seen that animal. The group's effective experience became vastly larger than any individual's lifetime.

Language as the first network

Every subsequent information technology — writing, printing, the telegraph, the internet — is an attempt to do what language does, but across greater distances and longer timescales. Language is the original network. All the others are extensions of it.

The agriculture trap

Around 10,000 BC, in the region we now call the Fertile Crescent — roughly modern Iraq, Syria, and Turkey — humans began deliberately cultivating plants and herding animals. Agriculture appeared independently in China, Mesoamerica, and several other locations over the following few thousand years.

We tend to think of agriculture as obvious progress. More food, more people, civilisation. But the immediate experience of the people who made this transition was, in many ways, worse than what came before.

Hunter-gatherers typically worked 4–6 hours a day to meet their nutritional needs, ate a varied diet, and left no lasting trace on the land. Early farmers worked 10–12 hours a day, ate a monotonous diet dominated by one or two grains, and were significantly shorter and less healthy than their ancestors — the fossil record is unambiguous on this. Grain-based diets cause tooth decay. Dense settlements breed epidemic disease. Farming is exhausting, repetitive, and tethering in a way that hunting is not.

So why did it happen? Not because farming was better for the individuals who adopted it. It happened because farming produces more calories per acre — which means more people per acre — which means farming communities out-competed and displaced hunter-gatherer communities, whether through violence, disease, or simple demographic pressure. Agriculture wasn't chosen. It won.

"Agriculture is the worst mistake in the history of the human race." — Jared Diamond, The Third Chimpanzee

Diamond is being provocative, but the core point stands: the adoption of agriculture was not an unambiguous improvement in human welfare. It was a trade — more people, less wellbeing per person — driven by competitive pressure between groups, not by individual choice. This pattern — technologies that benefit the group or the powerful at the expense of the individual — will recur throughout this guide.

Surplus changes everything

The one thing agriculture did that hunting couldn't was create storable surplus. A grain harvest can be kept for months or years. That single fact rearranged everything.

Surplus means you can feed people who don't grow food. The moment you can do that, you have priests, soldiers, scribes, craftsmen, merchants, administrators. You have division of labour. You have cities. You have the state.

And you have, for the first time, something worth stealing. Hunter-gatherer bands raid each other, but there's a limit to how much you can take from people who carry everything they own. A grain store is different. Protecting it requires organisation. Raiding it requires organisation. Controlling access to it is the original form of economic and political power.

Specialisation and its discontents

Surplus enables specialisation, and specialisation turbocharged technology. When a potter does nothing but make pots, for decades, across a lifetime, the quality of pots improves in ways it never could when pot-making was a part-time activity. The same is true for every craft: metalworking, weaving, boat-building, bread-making.

But specialisation also creates dependency — and dependency creates hierarchy. The farmer depends on the smith for tools. The smith depends on the farmer for food. The merchant connects them and takes a cut. The soldier protects both and takes a cut. The priest legitimises the whole arrangement and takes the biggest cut of all. Specialisation is the engine of technical progress and the foundation of inequality, simultaneously, inseparably.

This is not an accident or a flaw. It's structural. Every technology that enables more specialisation also concentrates the benefits of that specialisation unevenly. The people who control access to the bottleneck — the grain store, the water source, the trade route, later the printing press, the server farm — capture a disproportionate share of the value that flows through it.

The bottleneck principle

Throughout this guide, ask yourself: what is the bottleneck this technology creates or removes? Whoever controls the new bottleneck captures the new value. The cotton gin, the railroad, the search algorithm — all of them shifted who held the bottleneck and therefore who captured the surplus.

Copper, bronze, iron: the metallurgy ladder

Copper was the first metal worked by humans, around 9,000 BC in the Fertile Crescent and the Balkans. It can be hammered cold into simple shapes — beads, pins, small tools. But copper is soft. A copper blade dulls quickly. It was valuable as jewellery and prestige goods, but it didn't transform warfare or agriculture.

Bronze did. Sometime around 3,300 BC, metalworkers discovered that mixing copper with tin produced an alloy dramatically harder than either metal alone. Bronze could hold an edge. A bronze sword beat a stone spear. Bronze tipped ploughs bit deeper into soil. The Bronze Age civilisations — Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Aegean, the Indus Valley — were built on this alloy.

But notice the geopolitics immediately encoded in this technology. Copper and tin are rarely found in the same place. The great Bronze Age civilisations were all centres of trade networks that brought these metals together across hundreds or thousands of kilometres. The collapse of the Bronze Age around 1,200 BC — one of the most dramatic civilisational collapses in history, taking down the Hittites, Mycenaean Greece, Egypt, and a dozen other states within a generation — was in part a supply chain failure. When the trade networks broke down, the bronze stopped flowing, the weapons dulled, the armies weakened.

European example
The Greek Bronze Age and its sudden end
Mycenae · ~1600–1200 BC

Mycenaean Greece — the civilisation behind the legends of Troy and Agamemnon — was a sophisticated bronze-dependent culture. The palace at Mycenae controlled extensive trading networks, maintained professional armies equipped with bronze armour and weapons, and kept detailed administrative records (the Linear B tablets, deciphered in 1952, turn out to be mostly accounting records — inventory lists for bronze, grain, and livestock).

Around 1200 BC, the palace system collapsed suddenly and completely. Within fifty years, the palaces were burned, the Linear B script forgotten, long-distance trade ceased, and population plummeted. Greece entered a 400-year "Dark Age." The cause is still debated — climate, invasion, internal revolt, supply chain collapse, or some combination — but the fragility is stark. A civilisation built on a complex, specialised technology and long-distance supply chains is also a civilisation that can collapse when those chains break.

Iron changed this calculus. Iron ore is effectively everywhere. It's not as easy to work as bronze — it requires much higher temperatures, and the techniques for producing hard iron (steel) took centuries to perfect — but once mastered, iron made high-quality metal tools and weapons accessible to any society that could build a good furnace. The iron-equipped armies of the Assyrians and later the Persians were nearly unstoppable. The democratisation of metal — anyone could mine it, anyone could smelt it — shifted power away from the trading-network controllers and toward whoever had the best technique and the most iron.

The wheel and the ship

The wheel appears around 3,500 BC in Mesopotamia, almost simultaneously with the first cities — which is not a coincidence. A wheel is only useful if you have roads, and roads are only worth building if you have something to move in volume. The wheel is a city technology. It presupposes a level of social organisation that could build and maintain infrastructure.

What the wheel did to the economy was straightforward and enormous: it cut transport costs. Moving goods by wheeled cart requires roughly one-tenth the energy of carrying them by hand. Reduced transport costs mean that trade becomes viable over longer distances, markets become larger, specialisation deepens, and prices fall. Every technology that reduces the cost of moving goods, information, or people has these downstream effects. The wheel, the ship, the railway, the internet — the logic is the same.

The ship extended the same logic across water. Mediterranean seafaring goes back at least 10,000 years — the island of Melos, a source of obsidian, was being traded across the Aegean before agriculture reached Greece. By 1,500 BC, Phoenician merchants were sailing the entire Mediterranean and beyond. By 600 BC, a Phoenician fleet circumnavigated Africa. The sea was not a barrier. It was a highway — faster, cheaper, and higher-capacity than any road.

Geography as technology

The Mediterranean is unusual: a large, calm, enclosed sea with hundreds of harbours and islands. It was almost purpose-built for maritime trade. The civilisations that grew up around it — Phoenician, Greek, Roman — were shaped by the affordances of that geography as much as by their own inventions. Geography is a form of inherited technology: it determines what's easy, what's hard, and what's impossible before you've built a single thing.

Writing: technology as memory

Writing was invented independently at least three times: in Mesopotamia around 3,200 BC, in China around 1,200 BC, and in Mesoamerica around 600 BC. Each time, it began the same way — not as literature or communication, but as accounting.

The earliest Sumerian tablets are receipts. "47 units of barley received from the storehouse." "23 sheep delivered to the temple." Writing was invented by bureaucrats, for bureaucrats, to track the flow of surplus. This should not be surprising. Writing solves the problem of memory at scale: when your city has 50,000 people and your temple economy is tracking thousands of transactions, human memory is not enough. You need an external memory system.

But writing quickly escaped its accounting origins. Once you have a system for externalising thought, people use it for everything: law, religion, narrative, science. Writing is the first technology that allows ideas to persist unchanged across centuries and travel unchanged across continents. An oral tradition drifts with each retelling. A written text — once fixed — is fixed. This gave writing enormous power to both preserve knowledge and freeze authority.

Literacy gradient
In every society that adopted writing, the ability to read and write was initially restricted to a small elite — priests, scribes, administrators. This created a power asymmetry: those who could read had access to law, religious texts, and trade contracts; those who couldn't were entirely dependent on literate intermediaries. Every new information technology recreates this gradient briefly, before it democratises. Writing stayed exclusive for millennia. The printing press broke it open in decades.

The first cities

Uruk, in southern Mesopotamia, is the first city we know of — perhaps 50,000 people by 3,000 BC. It had monumental temples, a professional army, a merchant class, a bureaucracy, and the world's first known written literature (the Epic of Gilgamesh, the oldest surviving story, is set here). It also had slaves, disease, crime, traffic, and sewage problems. Cities are, from the beginning, both the summit of human achievement and a deeply uncomfortable way to live.

What cities did to technology is difficult to overstate. A city concentrates people, ideas, and demand. A potter in a village of 200 might make twenty different customers happy. A potter in a city of 50,000 can specialise in a single style, type, or technique and still have more customers than they can serve. This selective pressure toward specialisation and quality drives technological improvement faster than any other factor.

Cities are also where ideas collide. The metallurgist living near the glassmaker might notice that the silica flux he uses has optical properties. The merchant who trades in both spices and textiles might spot an arbitrage that no one in either trade could see. Proximity creates combination, and combination is the engine of invention.

Real example
Athens: the world's first innovation cluster
Athens · ~500–300 BC

Classical Athens at its peak had perhaps 300,000 people in the wider city-state, with 30,000–40,000 adult male citizens eligible to participate in government. It was, by ancient standards, genuinely urban and genuinely diverse — Athenian markets included merchants, craftsmen, and intellectuals from across the Mediterranean world.

The results were extraordinary. In the space of roughly 150 years, Athens produced Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, Thucydides, Sophocles, Euripides, Aristophanes, Phidias, and dozens of others who shaped Western thought for two millennia. This was not because Athenians were smarter than other Greeks — Sparta and Corinth produced no such list. It was because Athens had the density, the freedom of argument, the surplus to support non-productive thinkers, and the cross-cultural contact that concentrates intellectual progress. Silicon Valley in the 20th century works for the same reasons Athens worked in the 5th century BC.

Greece and the thinking machine

The Greeks did not invent most of the technologies in this chapter — the Mesopotamians, Egyptians, and Chinese got there first in most cases. What the Greeks invented was a technology for generating new knowledge: systematic rational inquiry. Philosophy, mathematics, geometry, logic, natural science — these are techniques for producing reliable knowledge about the world, and they are as much technologies as a bronze sword or a grain store.

The Greek achievement was to notice that the world has regular patterns — that the same cause reliably produces the same effect — and that those patterns can be investigated systematically rather than explained by story and myth. Thales of Miletus, around 600 BC, is credited as the first person to explain natural phenomena without reference to the gods. He was wrong about almost everything specific (he thought everything was made of water), but the move he made — naturalistic explanation, not supernatural — was revolutionary.

What did this do to technology? In the short run, not much. Greek science was largely theoretical. The Romans, who conquered Greece, absorbed Greek philosophy but were less interested in theory than in engineering — aqueducts, roads, concrete. But in the long run, the Greek framework for rational inquiry became the foundation on which the Scientific Revolution of the 17th century was built. The technology of systematic thinking has a very long payoff period.

Why Greece and not Egypt or Babylon?

Egypt and Babylon had more advanced mathematics than Greece for most of the first millennium BC. But both were centralised, hierarchical societies in which knowledge was the property of priestly castes, kept secret, and used to maintain authority rather than shared and debated. Greek city-states, particularly democratic Athens, created public spaces — the agora, the theatre, the symposium — where ideas were contested openly. The debate culture of Greece is not separate from its intellectual achievements. It is the explanation for them. Open, contested intellectual culture produces better knowledge than closed, authoritative culture. This lesson also has a very long payoff period.

The pattern you'll keep seeing

We've covered three million years in thirteen sections, which means we've skipped an enormous amount. But the patterns that matter are already visible. Let's name them, because you'll need them for every chapter that follows.

Technology is shaped by conditions, not the other way around. Fire, agriculture, bronze, writing — none of these happened because someone sat down and decided to invent something. They happened because conditions made them possible and useful: available materials, existing knowledge, economic incentives, social organisation. The lone genius inventor is almost always a myth. Technologies emerge from ecosystems.

Every technology creates a new bottleneck. The grain store. The bronze supply chain. The literacy gradient. Whoever controls the bottleneck captures the surplus. This is not a bug; it's a structural feature of how value flows around a new capability. The question is always: who gets to be the bottleneck?

Technologies that scale bring both progress and pain. Agriculture produced civilisation and also slavery, epidemic disease, and 12-hour days. Bronze enabled trade networks and also imperial conquest. Writing created law and also propaganda. The benefits and the harms are not separable — they come from the same source.

Dense, open, interconnected societies innovate faster. Athens outperformed Sparta. The Mediterranean cities outperformed the steppe. The campfire group outperformed the lone hunter. Every time you see an explosion of innovation, look for density plus openness plus cross-cultural contact.

"We shape our tools, and thereafter our tools shape us." — Marshall McLuhan

In the next chapter we jump 3,000 years — to the period between 0 and 1500 AD, an era usually dismissed as static, which turns out to be anything but. The printing press, eyeglasses, the mechanical clock, the stirrup, the windmill — the foundations of modernity were laid quietly in what gets called the Dark Ages. The tape isn't rewinding. It's accelerating.
