Energy Decoded · Chapter 04

Electricity
The Invisible River

Every other energy source in history was about making heat. Electricity was something fundamentally different — a force that could be sent anywhere, used for almost anything, and controlled with a switch.

On the evening of 4 September 1882, Thomas Edison threw a switch in a small building at 257 Pearl Street in lower Manhattan. Across a small district of New York City, 400 electric lamps flickered on. The Pearl Street Station — the world's first commercial central power station — had gone live.

It served 59 customers and 400 lamps across one square mile of New York. It ran on six massive steam-driven dynamos burning coal. By most measures, it was comically small-scale. But within twenty years, electrical grids would span continents. Within fifty, they would power every hospital, factory, and home in the industrialised world. Within a hundred, life without electricity would become, for much of humanity, simply unimaginable.

Electricity is fundamentally different from every previous energy source in human history. Fire gives you heat. Coal gives you heat. Oil gives you heat and mechanical motion. But electricity — electricity can do almost anything. It can create light without combustion. It can drive precise motors at exact speeds. It can transmit information as well as power. It can be switched on or off in milliseconds. It can be generated from almost any energy source — falling water, wind, burning coal, splitting atoms — and delivered anywhere a wire can reach.

What Electricity Actually Is

Definition — Electrical Power

Electricity, as we use it industrially, is the flow of electrons through a conductor — a wire. This flow is driven by a difference in electrical potential (voltage) between two points, produced by a generator. Generators work by moving a conductor (usually copper coils) through a magnetic field, which induces electron flow. The power in the circuit is measured in watts (voltage × current). The energy consumed over time is measured in kilowatt-hours (kWh). The crucial distinction from other energy forms: electricity is not a fuel. It is an energy carrier — it must be generated from some other source (falling water, burning fuel, nuclear fission, wind) and then transmitted to where it is needed.

This distinction — electricity as carrier, not source — is the key to understanding both its versatility and its limitations. A pipeline carries oil from wellhead to user. A wire carries electrical energy from generator to user. In both cases, the wire or pipe itself doesn't contain energy; it transmits it. But electricity travels at nearly the speed of light and can be converted to and from other energy forms — light, heat, motion, sound, computation — with extraordinary efficiency. No other energy carrier can do this.

The 360° Context: The World in the 1880s

🌍 Geopolitical

The 1880s were the peak of European colonial expansion. The Berlin Conference (1884–85) was carving up Africa. Britain, France, Germany, and the US were competing for industrial and imperial supremacy. Whoever mastered the new electrical technology would have a decisive advantage.

🏭 Industrial

Steam power was approaching its mechanical limits. The factory system needed more precise control than steam could offer. Electric motors could be sized for individual machines, started and stopped independently, and run at controlled speeds — a revolution in manufacturing flexibility.

🌆 Urban

Cities were growing beyond what gas lighting could serve. Urban fires from gas leaks were a constant hazard. The demand for safe, bright, controllable lighting in homes, shops, and streets was enormous and commercially irresistible.

💰 Financial

Industrial capitalism was generating enormous pools of investment capital seeking new opportunities. The electrical industry offered exactly what speculative capital craved: high upfront infrastructure costs, massive scaling potential, and near-monopolistic network advantages.

🔬 Scientific

Faraday, Maxwell, and Hertz had laid theoretical groundwork for electromagnetism. The 1880s was the moment theory met commercial application. For the first time, a generation of inventors could translate scientific principles directly into products at industrial scale.

🧠 Human Behaviour

People in 1880 woke with the sun and went to bed not long after dark. Candles and gas lamps were expensive, limited, and dangerous. The desire to extend productive and social hours beyond sunset — already demonstrated by coal-town factory schedules — was a powerful social force waiting for a better solution.

The War of the Currents

The first great business war of the electrical age was fought not over markets in the conventional sense, but over the fundamental design of electrical infrastructure. It is known as the War of the Currents, and it remains one of the most instructive case studies in how technical standards get decided — not by physics, but by money, personality, and politics.

Thomas Edison had built his system around direct current (DC) — electricity flowing in one direction, at constant voltage. DC was well understood, safe to work with, and Edison's business interests were deeply invested in it. But DC had a critical limitation: it couldn't be efficiently transmitted over long distances. A DC power station could serve customers within about one mile. Beyond that, the voltage dropped too much to be useful.

Nikola Tesla, a Serbian-born inventor who had briefly worked for Edison before a falling out over unpaid fees, had a different vision. Alternating current (AC) — electricity that reverses direction many times per second — could be "stepped up" to high voltages for long-distance transmission, then "stepped down" for safe household use, using a simple device called a transformer. AC could serve customers hundreds of miles from the generating station.

Real Story — Edison's Smear Campaign

When George Westinghouse, Edison's main commercial rival, adopted Tesla's AC system, Edison launched one of history's first corporate smear campaigns. To prove AC was dangerous, Edison and his team publicly electrocuted animals — dogs, calves, and eventually, most notoriously, a circus elephant named Topsy — with Westinghouse's AC current. Edison lobbied for AC to be used in the electric chair for executions, hoping "death by Westinghouse" would permanently associate AC with danger and death.

The campaign failed spectacularly. Westinghouse won the contract to light the 1893 Chicago World's Fair with AC, demonstrating its practicality to millions of visitors. He then built the first large-scale hydroelectric power station at Niagara Falls, transmitting AC power 22 miles to Buffalo, New York. Edison's DC system could never have done this. The War of the Currents was over, and Tesla's AC had won. The infrastructure of the modern electrical grid — AC transmission at high voltages, stepped down for local use — is exactly what Tesla envisioned in the 1880s.

The Grid: Infrastructure as Civilisation

The electrical grid is the most complex machine ever built. By the mid-20th century, the interconnected power grids of the United States, Europe, and other industrialised regions had become single, continent-spanning systems in which power generated at any point could flow to any other point, balanced in real time to match supply and demand to within fractions of a percent.

What is easy to forget is that building the grid required not just engineering but social reorganisation on a massive scale. The electrification of rural America, for example, required the 1936 Rural Electrification Act — because private companies had no profit incentive to wire areas too sparsely populated to return their investment. The federal government had to step in, creating rural electric cooperatives that still operate today. Similar patterns repeated across Europe and the developing world: electrical infrastructure required state action because the market alone would not build it.

Insight — Electrification Changed the Body

One of the least discussed effects of electrification is what it did to human health and labour. Electric pumps brought clean water into homes. Electric refrigerators eliminated the need for daily food shopping and dramatically reduced food poisoning. Electric washing machines — still, in the 1950s, primarily hand-operated in most homes — began to be replaced by powered machines, liberating millions of primarily women from hours of physical drudgery per week. The reduction in infant mortality and the increase in female participation in education and work in the 20th century are both significantly attributable to electrification. Energy transition, at this level, is not about infrastructure — it is about social transformation.

The Greek Electrical Story: DEI and the Island Problem

Greece's relationship with electrical infrastructure is a fascinating edge case that illuminates problems much of the developing world faces. Greece is a country of mountains, peninsulas, and islands — 227 of which are inhabited. This geography made building a unified national grid enormously expensive and technically complex.

The Public Power Corporation (DEI — Δημόσια Επιχείρηση Ηλεκτρισμού) was founded in 1950, two years after the end of the Greek Civil War, as the country tried to rebuild from a decade of occupation and conflict. DEI embarked on a massive hydroelectric and lignite-powered programme that, over the following thirty years, connected virtually every Greek community to the grid — including remote mountain villages and mainland-adjacent islands.

Real Story — The Greek Islands and the Submarine Cable Challenge

The smaller Greek islands presented a particular problem. Too small to justify their own power stations, too remote to connect easily to the mainland — for decades, many islands ran on expensive diesel generators, with electricity costing several times the mainland rate. Tourists would experience power cuts. Businesses couldn't scale. Cold chains for food and medicine were unreliable.

The solution was submarine power cables — technology that is expensive and technically demanding but can deliver mainland grid power to islands. Greece invested heavily in these connections from the 1990s onwards, eventually connecting the Cyclades, Dodecanese, and Cretan grids to the mainland. Crete — the largest Greek island, with a population of around 650,000 — completed its high-voltage mainland interconnection in 2021, finally bringing it fully into the national grid. The practical impact was immediate: power reliability improved, electricity prices for consumers fell, and the island became capable of hosting large-scale renewable energy development. The lesson: even in a small country, geography can delay energy inclusion by decades.

Electricity and the Digital Revolution

Everything in this chapter so far has been about electricity as a physical infrastructure for power. But in the second half of the 20th century, electricity became something else: the medium for information. The transistor (1947), the integrated circuit (1958), and the microprocessor (1971) were all discoveries about how to use electricity to represent, process, and transmit information at unimaginable speeds and scales.

The digital revolution is, at its foundation, an electrical revolution. Every smartphone, server, data centre, and internet cable runs on electricity. The global internet consumes approximately 200–250 terawatt-hours of electricity per year — roughly equal to the entire electricity consumption of Iran. Training a large AI language model can consume as much electricity as 100 average US homes use in a year. The explosion of data centres driven by cloud computing and AI is projected to be one of the largest drivers of new electricity demand through the 2030s.

"The story of the 21st century will be told in kilowatt-hours. Every thing we build, communicate, or compute depends on electricity. The question is no longer whether we need more of it — but where it comes from."

— Energy analyst paraphrase, IEA World Energy Outlook 2023

The Great Sea Interconnector: Greece as Energy Crossroads

Greece sits at a geographically and energetically strategic location: between the sun-rich North African desert, the wind-swept Aegean, and the major electricity markets of Central Europe. This geography is increasingly being recognised as a major economic asset.

The Great Sea Interconnector — a proposed 1,200km submarine high-voltage direct current (HVDC) cable linking Israel, Cyprus, and Greece, and connecting to the European grid — would, if completed, be the longest and deepest submarine power cable ever built. It would allow large-scale Israeli and Cypriot solar generation to supply European demand, while giving Greece a key role as a transit hub for Mediterranean renewable electricity. For a country that spent decades dependent on imported oil and its own lignite, this represents a remarkable geopolitical reversal: Greece as energy exporter rather than importer.

Electricity's Unsolved Problem: Storage

For 140 years, the electrical grid has operated on a single ruthless constraint: supply and demand must be balanced at every moment, across every part of the grid, or the system fails. Frequency deviations — caused by supply and demand going out of balance — can, within seconds, cascade into grid collapse. The entire eastern US grid blacked out in 2003 because a software bug in Ohio allowed a small imbalance to cascade. Within eight minutes, 55 million people lost power.

The reason this matters for energy transition is fundamental: renewable energy is intermittent. The sun doesn't shine at night. The wind doesn't always blow when demand peaks. For renewables to replace fossil fuels, the grid needs either massive long-distance interconnection (so when it's dark in Germany, Saharan solar can fill the gap) or large-scale energy storage. Both are technically feasible. Both are enormously expensive. Both are the central challenge of the energy transition we explore in later chapters.
