The investment universe

Every path your money can take. From a checking account to a venture capital fund, with everything in between examined honestly — what it is, how it works, what it returns, and who it's actually for.

~55 min read 15 sections The complete menu

The fundamental fork: lending vs owning

Beneath the bewildering variety of financial products, there are only two ways money has ever made more money:

Path A: You lend it. You give your money to someone — a bank, a government, a company — and they promise to pay it back, with interest. You become a creditor. Your upside is fixed at the agreed interest rate. Your downside is that the borrower might default. If they go bankrupt, you have first claim on their assets, ahead of owners.

Path B: You own a piece of something. You buy a slice of a business, a chunk of property, a quantity of gold. You become an owner. If the thing makes money or appreciates, you share in the gains. If it loses money or declines, you share in the losses. Your upside is potentially unlimited. Your downside is potentially total. If the business goes bankrupt, owners get whatever's left after creditors are paid — usually nothing.

Every investment product, no matter how exotic, is fundamentally some version of one of these two things, or a combination. A bond is lending. A stock is owning. A mutual fund is a basket of lending or owning. An option is a contract on owning or lending. A derivative is a bet on the price of lending or owning.

Hold this fork in mind as we walk through the menu. It will keep you oriented.

Cash & cash equivalents: the safe and the safe-seeming

Cash is the most familiar form of money — paper bills, coins, and the balances in your checking and savings accounts. Beyond cash itself, there's a category called cash equivalents: investments so liquid and short-term that they're essentially the same thing.

Checking and savings accounts

Money you can access at any time. Pays very low interest (often 0% on checking; 0.1–4% on savings depending on the country and current interest rate environment). Deposit insurance covers up to €100,000 per bank per depositor in the EU, $250,000 in the U.S. Above those limits, you're an unsecured creditor of the bank — meaning if the bank fails, you join the queue.

Time deposits (CDs in the U.S., προθεσμιακές καταθέσεις in Greek)

You agree to leave the money for a fixed period — say, 12 months. In exchange, you get slightly higher interest. Withdraw early and you usually forfeit some or all of the interest. Insurance limits same as above.

Money market funds

Mutual funds that hold extremely short-term, high-quality debt: government bills, top-rated corporate paper, bank deposits. They aim to maintain a stable €1.00 per share value while paying interest close to the central bank's rate. In normal times, very safe. In crisis (2008), some "broke the buck" — fell below €1.00 per share — though European money market funds have generally held up.

Treasury bills (T-bills)

Very short-term government debt (under 1 year). About as safe as money gets in dollar or euro terms. Available in the U.S. as 4-week, 13-week, 26-week, and 52-week bills. In Europe, similar instruments are issued by each treasury.

The point of cash: Your emergency fund. Money you might need in the next 1–2 years. The dry powder for buying assets after a crash. Not long-term savings — for that, cash is a slow-bleeding wound, as Chapter 2 covered.

Bonds: lending money, formalized

A bond is a formal IOU. You give the issuer money up front. They pay you a fixed interest payment ("the coupon") at regular intervals — typically every 6 months — and return your original investment ("the principal" or "face value") on a specified date ("maturity").

For example, a 10-year German government bond ("Bund") with a face value of €1,000 and a 2.5% coupon: you pay €1,000 today, receive €25 per year for 10 years, then receive your €1,000 back at maturity. Total received: €1,250.

Sounds simple. The complications come from two facts: bonds can be sold before maturity at fluctuating prices, and not every borrower repays.

Why bond prices move

Once issued, bonds trade on secondary markets. If your 2.5% bond looks unattractive because new German bonds are now paying 4%, the price of your bond falls until its yield-to-maturity matches what new bonds offer. Conversely, if new bonds drop to 1%, your 2.5% bond becomes more valuable and its price rises.

This is the fundamental inverse relationship: bond prices fall when interest rates rise, and vice versa. The longer the maturity of the bond, the more sensitive it is to interest rate changes — a concept formalized as duration. A 30-year bond can lose 20% of its market value if rates rise just 1%; a 2-year bond barely moves.

The bond family

Government bonds (sovereign bonds). Lending to a country. Considered the safest debt in each currency zone — though some governments are safer than others. The U.S. Treasury is the global benchmark for "risk-free." German Bunds play that role in euros. Greek government bonds (παρά Τράπεζας της Ελλάδος) trade at a meaningful spread above Bunds because of higher perceived risk. Maturities range from a few months (T-bills) to 30+ years.

Investment-grade corporate bonds. Lending to creditworthy companies — rated AAA down to BBB by agencies like Moody's, S&P, and Fitch. Pay a spread above government bonds (typically 0.5–2 percentage points) reflecting default risk. Apple bonds, Nestlé bonds, BMW bonds, etc.

High-yield bonds (junk bonds). Lending to companies with weaker credit — rated BB or below. Pay much higher spreads (3–8 percentage points above government bonds) reflecting much higher default risk. Default rates in this category run 2–4% per year in normal times, much higher in recessions. Suitable for diversified portfolios, not for individual holdings.

Inflation-linked bonds. The principal and coupons adjust upward with inflation. The U.S. version is called TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities). The French version is OATi. The German version is iBund. These are useful for hedging unexpected inflation but tend to pay lower "real" rates than nominal bonds.

Convertible bonds. Corporate bonds that can be converted into a fixed number of the company's shares. They pay a lower coupon than regular bonds in exchange for the equity upside. A hybrid between debt and equity.

Municipal bonds (muni bonds). Bonds issued by local/regional governments. In the U.S., interest is often tax-free, which makes them attractive for high-income earners. Less prevalent in Europe.

Asset-backed securities (ABS) and mortgage-backed securities (MBS). Bonds whose payments come from a pool of underlying loans — mortgages, car loans, credit card debt. These were at the center of the 2008 crisis. Complex; not for individual investors.

The yield curve and what it tells you

If you plot government bond yields at every maturity from 1 month to 30 years, you get a curve. Normally, longer maturities pay higher yields (you should be rewarded for tying up your money longer). When the curve inverts — when short-term yields exceed long-term yields — it has historically been one of the most reliable predictors of recession in the next 6–18 months.

Where bonds fit

Bonds traditionally play two roles in a portfolio: they generate income (the coupons), and they tend to rise when stocks fall (because in a crisis, investors flee to safety). The second role broke down badly in 2022 when both stocks and bonds fell together, but in most historical crises it has held.

The realistic expected real return (after inflation) on a high-quality bond portfolio is usually 1–3%. Not exciting. But the diversification benefit is real, particularly during recessions.

Stocks: what a share actually is

A share of stock is a tiny fractional ownership in a real business. If Apple has approximately 15 billion shares outstanding and you own one, you own one-fifteen-billionth of Apple. You have legal claim to:

  • A proportional share of Apple's profits (paid as dividends, when the board chooses to)
  • A proportional share of Apple's assets if Apple is liquidated (after creditors)
  • A proportional vote at shareholder meetings (usually one vote per share)

That's it. That's what a share is. Everything else — the stock's daily price, the market sentiment, the analyst targets — is commentary on what people are willing to pay for that fractional ownership at any given moment.

The two ways a stock makes you money

Dividends. The company pays you a portion of its profits, usually quarterly. Mature, profitable companies (Coca-Cola, Procter & Gamble, Unilever, Nestlé) often pay 2–4% of their stock price per year in dividends. High-growth companies typically pay no dividends, preferring to reinvest profits.

Capital gains. You sell the share later for more than you paid. This is where most stock returns come from over long periods. Apple stock at $0.10 in 1980, $200+ in 2024 — pretty much none of that return came from dividends.

Over very long periods (100+ years), broad stock markets have produced roughly:

  • ~10% nominal annual returns in the U.S.
  • ~7% real (inflation-adjusted) annual returns
  • About half from dividends, half from capital gains, historically — though the dividend share has shrunk in recent decades as companies prefer buybacks

These are averages over very long periods. Any individual year can be wildly different.

How stocks are priced (the basics)

A stock's price reflects the market's collective guess about what the company will earn in the future, discounted back to today using prevailing interest rates. A few key valuation ratios:

  • P/E (Price/Earnings): stock price divided by annual earnings per share. The most common valuation measure. Historic average for the U.S. market is around 15–17. A P/E of 30 means investors are paying €30 today for €1 of current annual earnings, betting earnings will grow rapidly.
  • P/B (Price/Book): price divided by the company's accounting book value. Useful for banks and capital-heavy businesses; less useful for tech companies with mostly intangible assets.
  • Dividend yield: annual dividend divided by stock price. Tells you the yield you get from dividends alone. Compares directly to bond yields.
  • P/S (Price/Sales): useful for companies that aren't yet profitable. A growing company might have a high P/S because investors expect future profitability.
  • EV/EBITDA: Enterprise Value divided by Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. The metric professionals favor because it accounts for debt and isn't distorted by accounting choices.

You can spend a career studying valuation. For most investors, the lesson is simpler: high valuation ratios indicate the market is optimistic; low ratios indicate pessimism. Buying when valuations are very high has historically led to lower future returns. Buying when valuations are very low has historically led to higher future returns. This is the cornerstone of "value investing" — though for stretches of years, it can look broken.

Stock styles: the categories within

Not all stocks behave alike. Five major axes divide the equity universe:

Market capitalization (size)

  • Large-cap — companies worth €10B+. Apple, LVMH, Nestlé, Siemens. Established, less volatile, slower-growing.
  • Mid-cap — €2B–€10B. Less coverage from analysts; sometimes overlooked but high-quality businesses.
  • Small-cap — €300M–€2B. Higher growth potential, higher volatility, sometimes less liquid.
  • Micro-cap — under €300M. Very high risk; often illiquid; can be ten-baggers or zeros.

Growth vs value

One of the oldest distinctions in investing.

  • Growth stocks: companies expected to grow earnings rapidly. Trade at high P/E ratios. Pay little or no dividend. Examples: Nvidia, Tesla, ASML in their growth phases.
  • Value stocks: companies trading at low multiples of earnings, book value, or sales — often because the market sees problems (real or imagined). Examples: many banks, energy companies, traditional industrials. Pay higher dividends.

Long-run data showed value stocks slightly outperforming growth stocks over the 20th century — the so-called "value premium." That premium reversed dramatically in the 2010s, with growth (especially U.S. tech) crushing value. Whether the historical pattern reasserts itself is a major academic debate.

Sectors (and cyclicals vs defensives)

Stocks are also categorized by industry: technology, financials, healthcare, energy, consumer discretionary, consumer staples, utilities, industrials, materials, communication services, real estate. Each sector behaves differently across the business cycle.

  • Cyclicals (consumer discretionary, financials, industrials, materials) — do well when the economy expands, badly when it contracts.
  • Defensives (consumer staples, healthcare, utilities) — perform similarly across cycles because people buy toothpaste, take medicine, and use electricity regardless of the economy.

Tactical investors try to rotate between sectors based on where in the cycle they think we are. Most fail at this. But understanding which sectors are which is useful for diagnosing your overall portfolio risk.

Geographic exposure

Where the company earns its revenue often matters more than where it's headquartered. Nestlé is Swiss but earns globally. Toyota is Japanese but a huge share of sales is in North America. For most globally diversified stocks, the headquarters matters less than you'd think.

Special categories worth knowing

Dividend aristocrats: U.S. companies in the S&P 500 that have raised their dividend every year for at least 25 consecutive years. Coca-Cola, Procter & Gamble, Johnson & Johnson, etc. Popular with income-focused investors.

Blue chips: vague term for very large, well-established, stable companies. Came from the highest-value gambling chip in poker.

Penny stocks: stocks trading below $1 (or €1). Most are essentially worthless companies whose stock has fallen to almost nothing, or shell companies used for fraud schemes. Avoid almost entirely. The pump-and-dump scams in The Wolf of Wall Street are exactly this market.

IPOs (Initial Public Offerings): a company's first sale of stock to the public. The mythology says IPOs make people rich. The reality: most IPOs underperform the broader market over the first 3–5 years. The first-day "pop" benefits the institutional buyers who got allocations, not the retail investors who pile in after. Avoid as a strategy.

SPACs (Special Purpose Acquisition Companies): shell companies that raise money via IPO, then go looking for a private company to merge with. A backdoor way to go public. Most SPACs from the 2020–2021 boom have lost 70%+ of their value. The structure is generally unfavorable to retail investors. Caution.

Mutual funds: the original collective investment

A mutual fund is a pool of money from many investors, used to buy a portfolio of securities chosen by a professional manager. You buy "shares" of the fund. The price (called Net Asset Value, or NAV) is calculated once per day after market close, based on the value of all the underlying holdings divided by the number of fund shares outstanding.

Mutual funds revolutionized investing in the 20th century by giving small investors access to diversified portfolios they could never have built individually. Two main flavors:

Active mutual funds

A manager (or team) actively picks stocks or bonds, trying to beat a benchmark (e.g., the S&P 500 or a euro stoxx index). They charge fees for this effort — typically 0.5–2.0% per year — called the expense ratio or TER (Total Expense Ratio).

The uncomfortable truth: most active funds, over long periods, fail to beat their benchmark after fees. The SPIVA reports (S&P Indices vs. Active) consistently show that over 15-year periods, 85–95% of active U.S. equity funds underperform their index. In European equity funds, the numbers are similar. International funds and emerging markets show slightly better active records but still poor.

Why? Markets are competitive. The "edge" required to consistently outperform is rare and hard to identify in advance. By the time a fund manager has built a track record proving skill, fees and fund growth tend to erode future returns. The exceptions exist (Renaissance Technologies, Berkshire Hathaway) but you usually can't access them.

Index mutual funds

Instead of trying to beat a benchmark, these funds simply track one — holding all (or a representative sample) of the securities in an index. The S&P 500 fund holds the 500 stocks in the S&P 500, in roughly the same proportions. The fund manager's job is purely operational: maintain the index weights, handle inflows/outflows, minimize tracking error.

Because there's no need for star managers, expensive research, or active trading, costs are tiny — often 0.04–0.30% per year. Over decades, this cost advantage compounds into enormous returns.

The argument for index funds, made most famously by John Bogle (founder of Vanguard), is roughly: "I don't know which active manager will outperform. Statistically, most won't. By owning the index, I'm guaranteed the market return minus a tiny fee. That beats the average active fund after fees by a meaningful margin." Decades of data have validated this argument.

Open-end vs closed-end

The mutual funds we've described so far are open-end: the fund issues new shares when investors buy in and redeems shares when they sell, all at the daily NAV.

A closed-end fund has a fixed number of shares that trade on a stock exchange like any other stock. Its market price can diverge from the underlying NAV, sometimes by 10% or more. Sophisticated investors sometimes buy closed-end funds when they trade at significant discounts to NAV.

ETFs: the modern revolution

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember this section. For most people, most of the time, ETFs are the answer.

What an ETF actually is

An ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund) is a fund that trades on a stock exchange like a single stock. You can buy and sell ETF shares throughout the trading day at constantly-updating market prices. ETFs typically track an index (like a S&P 500 ETF tracks the S&P 500), giving you instant diversification across all the index's holdings in a single trade.

The first ETF (SPY, tracking the S&P 500) launched in 1993. There are now over 8,000 ETFs globally, with about €10 trillion in total assets. The category has eaten the mutual fund industry, particularly for index investing.

Why ETFs beat mutual funds for most people

  1. Lower costs. ETF expense ratios for major index ETFs are often 0.03–0.15% per year, lower than even index mutual funds in many cases.
  2. Tax efficiency. Due to a quirk of ETF structure (the creation/redemption mechanism, explained below), ETFs typically distribute less in taxable capital gains than mutual funds.
  3. Intraday trading. You can buy or sell at any time during market hours, at the current market price. (For long-term investing, this doesn't matter much — but it's a feature.)
  4. Transparency. Most ETFs publish their holdings daily. Most mutual funds disclose holdings only quarterly.
  5. Low minimums. You can buy a single share, sometimes worth €5–€500. Mutual funds often have minimum investments of €1,000+ or more.

How the magic works (creation/redemption)

The reason ETF prices stay very close to their underlying NAV is an elegant arbitrage mechanism. Large institutional players called "Authorized Participants" can either:

  • Hand a basket of the underlying stocks to the ETF provider in exchange for new ETF shares (creation), or
  • Hand ETF shares to the provider in exchange for the underlying stocks (redemption).

If ETF shares trade at a premium to NAV, APs create new ETF shares (selling them on market, pocketing the premium, pushing the price down toward NAV). If they trade at a discount, APs redeem ETF shares (collecting the underlying stocks worth more than the ETF's market price). This constant arbitrage keeps ETF prices very close to NAV.

The ETF taxonomy

"ETF" is a structure, not an investment strategy. Within the ETF wrapper, there are many varieties:

Broad-market index ETFs. Track the whole market or major sub-segments. Examples by category: a "global all-cap" ETF (giving you 8,000+ stocks worldwide), an "S&P 500" ETF (500 U.S. large-caps), a "MSCI World" ETF (1,500+ stocks across developed markets), a "FTSE Emerging Markets" ETF. For most people, this is the only category that really matters.

Sector ETFs. Concentrate on one industry: technology, healthcare, financials, energy, etc. Useful for tactical tilts but counterproductive for most long-term investors.

Country/region ETFs. Single-country exposure: Germany, Japan, India, Brazil. Useful for specific bets, dangerous as a long-term core holding.

Thematic ETFs. Concentrated bets on specific themes: clean energy, AI, cybersecurity, robotics, water, lithium, cannabis, the metaverse. Almost always launched after a theme has already become popular (and prices have run up). The track record of thematic ETFs as a category is poor — they tend to peak shortly after launching and disappoint thereafter. The marketing is exciting; the returns rarely are.

Smart-beta / factor ETFs. Try to capture historically rewarded "factors" — value, momentum, quality, low volatility, small size — through systematic rules. The academic basis is real (factor investing has decades of research). Real-world implementation often disappoints due to costs and crowding. Reasonable for sophisticated investors; unnecessary complexity for most.

Bond ETFs. Provide exposure to bond portfolios with daily liquidity. Government bond ETFs, corporate bond ETFs, high-yield bond ETFs, aggregate bond ETFs (mixing types), TIPS ETFs for inflation protection. Among the easiest ways for individual investors to access fixed income.

Dividend ETFs. Hold baskets of high-dividend stocks. Popular with income-focused investors. Caveat: chasing high yields can lead you to lower-quality companies whose high yield reflects market doubts about the dividend's sustainability.

Commodity ETFs. Track gold, silver, oil, agricultural commodities. Some hold physical commodities (gold and silver); others use futures contracts (most other commodities). The futures-based ones can suffer from "contango" — losing value as they roll expiring contracts forward — which makes them poor long-term holds.

Active ETFs. A new category where ETFs hold actively-managed portfolios. Most are too new to have meaningful track records. The same skepticism about active management generally applies.

Leveraged ETFs. Use derivatives to amplify daily returns 2x or 3x. They're designed for day-traders, not long-term holders. Over time, the daily-rebalancing math systematically erodes value, especially in volatile markets. A 3x leveraged S&P 500 ETF held for years can lose value even if the underlying index goes up, due to "volatility decay." Most retail investors who buy leveraged ETFs lose money; warning labels exist for a reason.

Inverse ETFs. Designed to go up when the market goes down. Same daily-rebalancing problem as leveraged ETFs. Not for long-term holding.

Critical ETF details for European investors

UCITS compliance. EU regulations require that retail investment products meet UCITS standards. Most U.S.-listed ETFs (the SPY, VOO, QQQ you read about in American articles) do not meet UCITS requirements. They're generally not available to EU retail investors. The equivalent UCITS-compliant ETFs are usually listed on European exchanges (London, Amsterdam, Frankfurt) and have similar tickers but different identifiers.

Distributing vs accumulating. European ETFs come in two flavors:

  • Distributing (Dist): pays dividends as cash, typically quarterly or annually. You pay tax on each distribution. Good if you want income or will spend the dividends.
  • Accumulating (Acc): reinvests dividends inside the fund automatically. The fund's NAV simply rises faster. Often more tax-efficient for long-term growth in many EU jurisdictions, though some countries (Germany, Austria) have specific rules. Verify tax treatment in your country.

For long-term wealth building, accumulating funds are usually the simpler default — you don't have to manually reinvest dividends or pay annual tax on them in some jurisdictions.

Physical vs synthetic replication. Most ETFs hold the actual underlying securities (physical replication). Some — particularly for indices that are expensive or hard to replicate (emerging markets, some commodities) — use swaps with banks to achieve the index's returns (synthetic replication). Synthetic ETFs introduce counterparty risk (the bank could fail) but UCITS rules limit this exposure. Physical is generally preferred when available.

Domicile and dividend tax. Most European ETFs are domiciled in Ireland (look for "IE" in the ISIN code) or Luxembourg ("LU"). Ireland-domiciled ETFs benefit from a 15% U.S. withholding tax on dividends from U.S. stocks, rather than 30% — meaningful compounding advantage over decades when investing in U.S. equities.

TER (Total Expense Ratio). The annual cost. For a broad global equity ETF, expect TER of 0.10–0.30%. For a major bond ETF, 0.05–0.20%. For a thematic ETF, 0.50–0.80% (one reason to be skeptical of them). Lower TER is reliably better — fees compound against you over decades.

Tracking error. How closely the ETF actually matches its index. For major indices, tracking error is usually negligible. For more obscure indices, it can matter.

AUM (Assets Under Management). ETFs with under €100M in assets sometimes get closed by their issuers. Sticking to ETFs with at least €500M (and preferably €1B+) reduces this risk.

The ETF in one sentence

For 90% of investors, a single low-cost, broadly-diversified accumulating UCITS equity ETF — like one tracking the MSCI All Country World Index or FTSE All-World — is the most powerful single tool ever created for ordinary people to participate in global capital. Combined with a bond ETF for stability, you have a complete portfolio.

Real estate: the asset class Europeans understand

Direct property ownership has been the dominant form of household wealth for most of history. It remains so in much of Europe, including Greece, where home ownership rates are far higher than in many other countries.

Direct ownership

Buying property — your home, a rental, a commercial building. Returns come from a combination of:

  • Rental yield: rent received minus expenses (maintenance, taxes, vacancy, management), divided by the property's value. In most European cities, gross yields are 3–6% before expenses; net yields 1–4%.
  • Capital appreciation: property values rising over time. Long-term, real estate has roughly tracked inflation in most countries, with occasional bubbles and crashes.
  • Leverage: this is where real estate becomes interesting. A €200,000 property bought with a €40,000 down payment plus a €160,000 mortgage gives you €200,000 of asset exposure for €40,000 of capital. If the property appreciates 5% (to €210,000), your equity grew from €40,000 to €50,000 — a 25% return on your money. Of course, leverage cuts both ways: a 20% fall in property value would wipe you out.

Real estate's strengths: leverage allows large positions on modest capital; rental income provides predictable cash flow; property is tangible and emotionally satisfying to own; many countries offer tax advantages for primary residences.

Real estate's weaknesses: illiquid (selling takes months); concentrated (one property is a huge bet on one location, one tenant base, one local economy); management-intensive (tenants, repairs, vacancies); transaction costs are high (5–10% to buy and sell); not easily diversifiable for most people.

REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts)

For real estate exposure without the headaches, REITs offer a paper version. A REIT is a company that owns and operates income-producing real estate — apartment complexes, office buildings, shopping centers, warehouses, data centers, cell towers, healthcare facilities. By law (in most jurisdictions), REITs must distribute at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders, giving them high dividend yields.

You can buy REITs as individual stocks (Vonovia in Germany, Unibail-Rodamco in France) or through REIT ETFs that hold dozens of REITs across categories. Dividend yields are typically 3–7%.

REITs trade more like stocks than like real estate — they're correlated with the broader stock market, especially during crises. They give you real estate's economic exposure but not its diversification benefits during a market crash.

Real estate funds and crowdfunding

Various structured products let you invest in real estate at smaller scale: real estate mutual funds, real estate crowdfunding platforms, fractional ownership schemes. Quality varies enormously. The crowdfunding category in particular has produced many disappointments — illiquid investments, opaque fees, properties that don't perform as advertised. Tread carefully.

Commodities: gold, oil, and everything that comes out of the ground

Commodities are raw materials — gold, silver, copper, oil, natural gas, wheat, corn, coffee, cattle. They're traded globally in standardized contracts.

The strange thing about commodities as investments

Unlike a stock (which represents an ongoing business that can grow and reinvest profits) or a bond (which pays interest), a commodity just sits there. A ton of copper does not produce more copper. A bar of gold does not pay dividends. The only way a commodity investment pays off is if the price rises.

Over very long periods, commodities have roughly tracked inflation — not much better. They have not matched the returns of stocks. This is partly because commodities pay no income while you hold them; partly because technology tends to make commodity extraction cheaper over time; partly because demand for individual commodities can be supplanted by substitutes.

So why hold them?

Gold (and precious metals)

Gold occupies a unique position. It has been money for 5,000 years. It has no industrial use that justifies its price (the small amount used in electronics doesn't move the needle). Its value comes entirely from collective agreement that it has value — which sounds familiar to readers of Chapter 1.

Gold tends to hold its real (inflation-adjusted) value over centuries. An ounce of gold bought a decent toga in Roman times; it buys a decent suit today. It is the ultimate anti-currency: when fiat currencies fail or wobble, gold often holds up. Periods of high inflation, geopolitical stress, or distrust in monetary authorities tend to be good for gold.

How to invest in gold:

  • Physical gold (coins, bars). The most direct exposure. Storage and insurance are headaches; verification of authenticity matters.
  • Gold ETFs (physical-backed, like the major ones in London and Zurich). The fund holds physical gold in a vault; your shares are claims on a fraction of it. The cleanest paper exposure.
  • Gold mining stocks. Operate as leveraged plays on gold prices but introduce company-specific risks (mismanagement, accidents, political risk in mining regions). Not pure gold exposure.

A 5–10% allocation to gold is defensible for risk-conscious portfolios. Larger allocations reflect deeper concerns about the monetary system.

Silver

Sometimes called "gold's volatile cousin." Has both monetary characteristics (like gold) and industrial uses (electronics, solar panels). Moves with gold but with larger swings. Less commonly held as a portfolio asset.

Energy (oil, gas)

The world runs on energy. Oil prices respond to geopolitical events (Middle East tensions, sanctions, OPEC decisions) and to demand cycles. Energy stocks (Shell, BP, TotalEnergies, ExxonMobil) provide indirect exposure with dividend income, but face long-term uncertainty as the energy transition progresses. Direct oil exposure via futures-based ETFs suffers from contango and is generally a poor long-term hold.

Industrial metals (copper, aluminum, lithium, etc.)

Driven by global industrial demand. Currently of particular interest due to the energy transition's enormous appetite for copper, lithium, nickel, and rare earths. Exposure through mining ETFs or major mining companies (BHP, Rio Tinto).

Agricultural commodities

Wheat, corn, soybeans, coffee, cocoa, sugar. Driven by weather, harvest cycles, geopolitical events. Volatile. Difficult to hold profitably long-term.

Cryptocurrencies: the youngest asset class

Bitcoin launched in 2009 as a novelty. By 2026, the crypto market is a real, if volatile, asset class with a total market capitalization in the trillions. It deserves serious treatment — neither dismissal nor evangelism.

Bitcoin

Bitcoin is, technically, the first instance of digital scarcity. Through cryptographic and game-theoretic design, the protocol guarantees that exactly 21 million bitcoins will ever exist (a hard cap reached approximately in 2140), and that ownership records cannot be altered by any central authority. It's a genuine technical achievement.

What Bitcoin is as an investment is much harder to say. It produces no income. It has no underlying business. Its value depends entirely on what other people are willing to pay. In this it's similar to gold — and indeed, the "digital gold" framing is the most coherent investment thesis for Bitcoin.

If Bitcoin ends up being adopted by a meaningful fraction of investors and institutions as a non-state store of value — as a kind of digital gold — its market cap could grow into the trillions and trillions. If it doesn't, it could fall by 90% or more. Both scenarios are alive in 2026.

A small allocation (1–5% of investable assets) for those who want exposure is defensible. A large allocation reflects either deep conviction or speculation. The volatility is real — Bitcoin has had multiple 80%+ drawdowns and many 50%+ rallies in its short history.

Ethereum

Second-largest cryptocurrency. Ethereum is a programmable blockchain — instead of just being digital money, it supports applications (called "smart contracts") that run on the network. DeFi (decentralized finance), NFTs, and many other crypto applications run on Ethereum.

The investment case is different from Bitcoin's. Ethereum is more like a tech infrastructure bet — if the ecosystem of applications running on it grows, the value of "ETH" (its native token) grows as users pay fees to use the network. The complexity is higher and the risks more idiosyncratic.

Stablecoins

Cryptocurrencies pegged to a stable asset, usually the U.S. dollar. The largest (Tether, USDC) maintain pegs by holding reserves of dollar-equivalent assets. Useful for crypto traders moving between assets without going back to traditional finance. Generally not investments per se — more like crypto-native dollars.

The risk: not all stablecoins are equally well-backed. The 2022 collapse of TerraUSD (UST) — an "algorithmic stablecoin" that lost its peg and went to zero — wiped out tens of billions in days.

Everything else

There are tens of thousands of other cryptocurrencies. Most are some combination of:

  • Genuinely interesting technical projects (a small minority)
  • Speculation tokens with no clear utility (most)
  • Outright scams (more than anyone wants to admit)

The historical attrition rate is brutal. Of the top 100 cryptocurrencies in 2017, only a handful remain in the top 100 today. Most went to near-zero. Picking individual altcoins is more like venture capital than investing — most go to zero; rare winners can be enormous.

DeFi and NFTs

Decentralized finance (DeFi) lets you lend, borrow, and trade crypto without traditional intermediaries. Some genuinely interesting innovations exist. The space is plagued by hacks (billions stolen annually), regulatory uncertainty, and complexity that makes errors costly. For sophisticated participants only.

NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens) — unique digital tokens representing ownership of specific digital items — were the speculative frenzy of 2021. The market collapsed in 2022–2023 and has not recovered. Treat the entire category as artistic-speculative collectibles, not investments.

Derivatives & leveraged products: where most retail investors lose money

Derivatives are financial contracts whose value derives from the value of something else — a stock, an index, a commodity, a currency, an interest rate. The major categories:

Options

A contract giving you the right (but not the obligation) to buy or sell an underlying asset at a specified price, by a specified date.

  • A call option gives you the right to buy. You profit if the underlying rises above the strike price plus what you paid for the option.
  • A put option gives you the right to sell. You profit if the underlying falls below the strike price.

Options have legitimate uses: hedging existing positions (buying puts on stocks you own to limit downside), generating income (selling covered calls on stocks you own), expressing complex views. They also have a death-trap quality for individual investors who use them as cheap leveraged bets on direction. Most options expire worthless. Most retail option traders lose money.

Futures

A contract obliging both parties to transact at a specified price on a specified future date. The contracts are standardized and trade on exchanges. Used by commercial operators (a farmer locking in next year's wheat price) and by speculators. Highly leveraged — small price moves can wipe out a position. Not for individual investors except through ETFs that wrap them.

CFDs (Contracts for Difference)

A bet on the price movement of an asset without owning it. Popular in Europe; banned for U.S. retail investors. Heavily marketed by trading platforms to retail investors as a way to "trade" stocks, indices, commodities, and forex with leverage.

The regulatory data is devastating. European regulators require CFD providers to publish the percentage of retail accounts that lose money. The numbers consistently show 70–85% of retail CFD accounts lose money. CFDs combine leverage (which amplifies losses), spreads/fees (which compound against you), and overnight financing costs (which bleed positions over time). They are wealth-destruction machines marketed as wealth-creation tools.

Forex (FX) trading

Speculating on currency exchange rates. Marketed heavily by online platforms. Same regulatory data: most retail traders lose money. The forex market is dominated by banks and institutional players with information and execution advantages that retail traders cannot match. Stay away.

Structured products

Complex packaged investments combining bonds, options, and other derivatives, designed to offer specific payoff profiles ("guaranteed minimum return plus upside") or yields enhanced by hidden risks. Sold by banks to retail clients. Almost always priced unfavorably to the buyer. Avoid unless you can fully decompose the product into its components and price each yourself.

Alternative investments: where the rich play

"Alternatives" is a vague category covering everything that isn't stocks, bonds, or cash. Most are inaccessible to ordinary investors due to minimums, regulations, or expertise requirements. A brief tour:

Private equity

Buying entire companies (or large stakes in them) that aren't publicly traded, improving operations, then selling them years later for a profit. Minimum investments typically €100,000+ for funds; some institutional vehicles require €5M+. Returns historically have been good but the gap with public markets has narrowed and fees are brutal (the classic "2 and 20" — 2% annual fee plus 20% of profits).

Venture capital

Investing in early-stage companies, mostly tech startups. Most VC investments fail (90%+ go to zero or underperform); a small minority return many multiples of original investment. The math relies on big winners. Top-tier VC funds have produced excellent returns; most have not. Access is the binding constraint — the good funds are closed to outsiders.

Hedge funds

Lightly regulated investment funds using various strategies — long/short equity, global macro, event-driven, statistical arbitrage. Originally a way to "hedge" market risk (hence the name); now a catch-all for any flexible fund structure. Fees are brutal (2 and 20). After fees, the average hedge fund has underperformed simple stock-bond portfolios for the past 20 years. The exceptional funds exist but are mostly closed to new investors.

Private credit

Direct lending to private companies, often as alternatives to bank loans. Has grown enormously as banks pulled back from middle-market lending after 2008. Higher yields than corporate bonds, but illiquid and exposed to default risk in recessions. Available to retail through some funds with high minimums.

Collectibles: art, wine, watches, classic cars

Tangible assets that can appreciate. Top-end fine art has produced strong returns for institutional collectors; the secondary market is opaque, transaction costs are huge (auction commissions 25%+), authenticity verification is critical. Wine and whiskey have hot periods; classic cars had a notable run in the 2010s. All require expertise; all are illiquid; most retail "investment" in collectibles is closer to expensive hobbies than to portfolio strategies.

For the rest of us

You can mostly ignore the alternatives category until you have at least €1M to invest, and even then most academic evidence suggests sticking with simple, low-cost public market portfolios. The mystique of alternatives is largely marketing.

Diversification: the only free lunch

The economist Harry Markowitz won a Nobel Prize for showing something that, in retrospect, seems obvious: by combining assets that don't all rise and fall together, you can reduce a portfolio's risk without reducing its expected return.

The mathematical key is correlation. If asset A returns 8% with 20% volatility, and asset B also returns 8% with 20% volatility, but they don't move together perfectly, a 50/50 portfolio of A and B will return 8% with less than 20% volatility. The less correlated A and B are, the bigger the volatility reduction.

Two assets that move in exactly opposite directions (correlation = −1) can in theory produce a risk-free portfolio. In practice, no two real assets have correlation −1, but bonds and stocks have historically had correlations around 0.0 to −0.3 in normal times (closer to 0 in inflation periods), which is enough to produce meaningful diversification.

The practical lessons:

  • Hold many different things, not just one or two.
  • Hold things that are economically different, not just nominally different (50 tech stocks isn't real diversification; you have one bet 50 times).
  • Diversify across asset classes (stocks AND bonds AND maybe real estate), geographies (your home country AND foreign markets), sectors (tech AND industrials AND consumer goods).
  • Recognize that during severe crises, correlations spike toward 1 — everything falls together for a few weeks or months. This means you can't fully diversify away risk, but you can reduce it.

For most individual investors, a global equity ETF + a bond ETF gets you 80% of the diversification benefit available. Adding REITs, gold, and small allocations to other asset classes captures the remaining 20%. Going beyond that adds complexity for diminishing returns.

How markets actually price things (and who's on the other side of your trade)

It's worth understanding, finally, why simply trying to "buy low, sell high" by picking individual stocks tends to fail for non-professionals.

The efficient market hypothesis, in its rough form, says that publicly available information is already reflected in stock prices, so you cannot reliably beat the market by analyzing public information. Every time you buy a stock thinking it's cheap, someone else is selling it thinking it's expensive. Most of the time, that someone is a sophisticated player with more information, faster execution, and better analytics than you.

Who are you trading against when you click "buy" on your brokerage app?

  • Quantitative hedge funds running models that process market data in microseconds
  • Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan, and other investment banks with teams of analysts
  • Mutual fund managers with Bloomberg terminals and direct access to company management
  • High-frequency trading firms whose servers are physically located in exchange data centers to shave microseconds off transactions
  • Corporate insiders (within legal limits) trading their own companies' stocks

These are the people you're trading against. They don't all win — but in aggregate, they have systematic information and execution advantages. The chance that you spotted something that all of them missed is low.

This is the deep reason why index investing works for most people. You're not trying to beat these professionals — you're sitting out the game and accepting the market's average return, which is itself excellent over long periods. Trying to beat the average is a different game, played by full-time professionals, and most players lose.

"Don't look for the needle in the haystack. Just buy the haystack." — John Bogle

The exception is when you have a genuine edge: deep expertise in a particular industry, inside knowledge (legal kind only, please), or a temperamental ability that others lack (e.g., the patience to hold through brutal drawdowns when others sell). For a few investors, these edges produce outperformance. For the vast majority, they don't exist.
