Chapter 01 · A People's History

How we got here

Every weird thing about money today — its forms, its fragility, its tendency to lose value — is the consequence of a specific decision made along the way. The story is the lesson.

~45 min read 20 sections From 9000 BC to today

Before money: the problem with trading goats for shoes

Imagine you make beautiful pottery. Your neighbor makes shoes. You want shoes. He doesn't want pottery — he wants wheat. The farmer who has wheat doesn't want pottery either — she wants chickens. The chicken farmer wants pottery, but only after the harvest. You wait. Your shoes wear through.

This is the coincidence of wants problem — the reason barter, despite what many economics textbooks claim, was never really how complex societies worked. Anthropologists have searched in vain for a society that ran on pure barter. They don't find them. What they find instead are elaborate systems of credit, gift exchange, and obligation — debts remembered across generations, payments deferred, communities woven together by webs of "I owe you, you owe me."

Money, when it arrived, didn't replace barter. It replaced this complex web of remembered obligations with something more portable: a thing that everyone agreed to accept in payment. A token of trust. A standardized IOU.

A useful first idea

Money is not a thing. Money is a relationship — between you and everyone else who agrees to accept what you hand them. The "thing" (coin, paper, digital number) is just the relationship made visible.

Shells, salt, and stones the size of small cars

The earliest moneys were stuff. Useful stuff, scarce stuff, durable stuff. Cowrie shells in China, India, and across Africa. Salt blocks in ancient Ethiopia and the Sahara (our word "salary" comes from the Latin salarium, a Roman soldier's salt allowance). Cattle in much of the ancient world (the word "pecuniary" comes from pecus, Latin for cattle). Cocoa beans among the Aztecs.

Each of these had similar properties: they were recognizable, hard to fake, durable, and roughly scarce enough that supply couldn't be conjured up overnight. They were also things you might actually want to own, which gave the system a floor: even if no one accepted your shells as money, you could still wear them.

Real example
The Rai stones of Yap
Micronesia · ongoing for centuries

On the island of Yap in the western Pacific, the local currency for centuries has been the rai: massive limestone discs, some twelve feet across and weighing several tons. They're so heavy they're never actually moved. When ownership changes hands, everyone on the island simply agrees that the stone now belongs to someone else. The stone might sit in the same field for a century, changing owners ten times.

Famously, one rai sank into the ocean during transport. The owner argued — and everyone agreed — that he still owned it. He could spend it. It worked exactly like any other rai. Just sitting underwater.

This is your first hint that money has always been more about agreement than substance. The Yapese, with their underwater stone, understood something modern economists are still arguing about.

The first coins (and why kings invented them)

Around 600 BC, in the kingdom of Lydia (in what is now western Turkey), King Alyattes or possibly his son Croesus stamped the first standardized coins from a natural gold-silver alloy called electrum. Each coin had a guaranteed weight and purity, marked with the king's stamp. You no longer had to weigh and assay each lump of metal in a transaction. You could just count coins.

This was a stunning innovation. It enabled trade across vast distances. Greek and Persian markets adopted it within a century. Within 300 years, coinage had spread across the Mediterranean and to India and China.

But notice what coinage really did: it concentrated power. Only the king could legitimately mint coins. Only the king's stamp was legal. Counterfeiting was punishable by death. And — crucially — the king could make a profit on the difference between the metal's value and the coin's stated value. This profit is called seigniorage, and it's been a feature of every monetary system since.

Seigniorage
The profit a money-issuing authority makes from issuing money. If a coin costs 5 cents of metal to produce but the king decrees it's worth 10 cents, the king pockets 5 cents per coin. Modern central banks do this too, in more sophisticated ways.

Rome: the first great inflation

For its first few centuries, Rome ran on a silver coin called the denarius. It was reliable. Soldiers were paid in it. Taxes were collected in it. The empire grew rich on it.

And then, slowly at first, Roman emperors discovered they could cheat. If a denarius was 100% silver, you could melt it down, mix in some copper, and turn one coin into two. The mark of the emperor was still there. People had to accept it as legal payment. The emperor could now pay twice as many soldiers, build twice as many roads, throw twice as many circuses.

Bronze coins with a metallic film. Prices in the empire had risen by 15,000% over the previous century. Diocletian issued an edict mandating maximum prices — the world's first wage-and-price control law. It failed.

What had happened? More coins were chasing the same amount of goods, so prices rose. Roman citizens caught on and started refusing the debased coins, or accepting them only at huge discounts. The empire's tax revenues — collected in increasingly worthless money — couldn't pay for the army. The army couldn't defend the borders. Provinces broke away. The western empire collapsed.

Inflation didn't single-handedly fall Rome. But it was a chronic illness that weakened the patient for centuries. This is the oldest pattern in monetary history: rulers debase the currency to spend more, citizens lose trust, the system breaks.

It's worth holding this image — copper coins with a thin silver wash that wears off — when you read Chapter 2 on modern fiat money. The mechanism today is sophisticated. The dynamic is identical.

The long road to paper money

For about 1,400 years after Rome, money in the West meant metal. Gold and silver coins, with their weight and purity guaranteed (or not) by whichever king or city-state minted them.

Paper money was invented elsewhere. In Tang dynasty China around the 7th century, merchants began leaving heavy strings of copper coins with trusted warehouses and carrying paper receipts instead. By the Song dynasty (about 1023 AD), the Chinese government had taken over the paper system and issued the world's first official paper currency: the jiaozi.

It worked beautifully — for a while. Then the government, like the Roman emperors with their silver, discovered it could simply print more paper whenever it needed funds. Inflation arrived. By 1455, after centuries of monetary chaos, paper money was abandoned in China and would not return for hundreds of years.

When Marco Polo returned to Venice in 1295 and described Chinese paper money to skeptical Europeans, they assumed he was making it up. Paper that was worth gold? Backed by nothing but the emperor's word? Impossible.

Europe wouldn't have widespread paper money until the 17th century, when goldsmiths in London — who held people's gold in vaults — began issuing paper receipts. These receipts could be passed from person to person; whoever held the paper could claim the gold. Eventually, people stopped bothering to redeem them. The paper itself became money. The goldsmiths, realizing not everyone would claim their gold at once, began issuing more receipts than they had gold to back. They had invented fractional reserve banking. We'll meet it again.

The Italians invent finance

Long before central banks and stock markets, the cities of medieval Italy — Florence, Venice, Genoa — invented most of the financial infrastructure we still use. They needed to. They were small republics surrounded by larger states, getting rich on trade with the Levant and northern Europe, and they had complex problems to solve.

How do you finance a trading voyage that won't return for two years? Equity partnerships — the commenda contract — let investors share in voyage profits proportional to their contribution. The ancestor of the modern joint-stock company.

How do you move money safely across hostile Europe without carrying gold that bandits would steal? Bills of exchange — paper letters that told a banker in another city to pay a specified sum to the bearer. The ancestor of the modern check and wire transfer.

How do you handle deposits, loans, and currency exchange at scale? Double-entry bookkeeping — codified by the Franciscan friar Luca Pacioli in 1494 — let banks track thousands of transactions with mathematical precision. It's still the basis of every accounting system in the world.

The Medici family of Florence used these tools to build the largest bank in Europe in the 15th century, with branches from London to Constantinople. They lent money to popes and kings (which was risky — kings have a habit of not repaying). They underwrote royal weddings. They financed the Renaissance. And when their loans to England's Edward IV went bad, the bank collapsed — proving that even the most sophisticated finance has always been vulnerable to a single big borrower deciding not to pay.

Spanish silver and the first global inflation

In 1545, the Spanish conquistadors discovered an entire mountain of silver at Potosí (in modern Bolivia). For the next century, an unimaginable flood of silver poured into Spain from the New World — by some estimates, 150,000 tons of it. Spain became, briefly, the wealthiest country in the world.

Then a strange thing happened. Prices across Europe began to rise. Not by a little — by 600% over the 16th century. Wages couldn't keep up. Workers got poorer even as the continent got richer. Spain itself, despite being the source of the silver, ended the century broke, having borrowed against future silver shipments to fund wars in the Netherlands and elsewhere.

Economists call this the Price Revolution. It was the first time anyone could clearly see the connection between the quantity of money in circulation and the level of prices — though the explanation, by the French philosopher Jean Bodin, wouldn't come until 1568. The Spanish silver had effectively diluted the value of every coin in Europe, just as Roman debasement had done thirteen centuries earlier, just as money printing does today.

The lesson echoed: more money chasing the same amount of goods means higher prices, full stop. Whether the new money is silver from a mountain, copper poured into denarii, paper from a printing press, or digits typed into a central bank's spreadsheet — the underlying force is the same.

Tulip mania: the original financial bubble

In the early 17th century, the Dutch Republic was the richest country in the world per capita. Tulips, recently imported from the Ottoman Empire, became a status symbol among the wealthy. Rare varieties — particularly the streaked ones (caused, we now know, by a tulip-specific virus) — could fetch large sums.

This was, in itself, normal. Status goods have always been expensive. What happened next was new.

By 1634, Dutch merchants had begun trading tulip contracts: paper rights to buy a bulb at a future date. The bulb didn't have to exist yet. You could buy and sell the contract many times before any tulip changed hands. By late 1636, ordinary craftsmen and laborers had borrowed money to speculate on these contracts. A single bulb of the rarest variety, Semper Augustus, was reportedly trading for the price of an Amsterdam canal house.

In February 1637 — over the course of a few days — buyers simply stopped showing up at an auction in Haarlem. The contracts plunged in value. People who had borrowed to speculate were ruined. Courts refused to enforce the contracts, deeming them effectively gambling debts.

The pattern
What every bubble has in common
1637 → forever

Tulip mania established the template every subsequent bubble follows. Phase one: a real underlying value (rare tulips really were beautiful). Phase two: increasing speculation, with new buyers attracted by rising prices rather than by the underlying value. Phase three: leverage — people borrowing to buy in. Phase four: novel financial instruments (contracts on contracts) that make speculation easier. Phase five: ordinary people, who don't normally invest, joining in. Phase six: a trivial event triggers a price drop. Phase seven: forced selling by leveraged buyers cascades into collapse.

You will see this pattern in 1720, in 1929, in 1989 (Japan), in 2000 (dot-com), in 2007 (housing), in 2017 (crypto), in 2021 (everything). The asset changes; the human story is identical.

The Bank of England: the birth of central banking

By 1694, England was at war with France (it would be, on and off, for the next 120 years), and the English crown was desperately short of money. A Scottish merchant named William Paterson proposed a deal: a group of wealthy lenders would loan the king £1.2 million. In return, they would be allowed to form a corporation — the Bank of England — with the privilege of issuing paper notes payable in gold.

This was a turning point in the history of money. The Bank of England was a private company, but its notes — backed by both the bank's gold reserves and its loans to the government — became the de facto national currency. The bank could create credit by issuing notes for more than it held in gold (the fractional reserve trick, again). The government had a permanent lender willing to fund its wars. The bank had a permanent customer willing to pay interest forever.

It was a marriage between sovereign power and financial power that has defined the modern world. Every major country eventually adopted some version of this model. The U.S. created the Federal Reserve in 1913. Germany had the Reichsbank, then the Bundesbank, then surrendered monetary sovereignty to the European Central Bank in 1999.

The exact arrangement varies, but the pattern is everywhere the same: a central bank, with monopoly over money creation, working hand-in-glove with the government, but technically independent enough to manage the money supply without political interference (in theory).

What this gave governments was the ability to spend without taxing — by borrowing from the bank, which would create the money to lend. What it gave the banking system was a permanent backstop: if a bank got into trouble, the central bank could lend it money out of thin air. What it gave citizens was a steadily-depreciating currency. There are no free lunches.

The year of bubbles: 1720

If 1637 was the original bubble, 1720 was the year bubbles went mainstream — and the year they convinced Europe's smartest people that they too could be ordinary fools.

In France, a Scottish economist named John Law had convinced the regent to give him a monopoly over French trade with the Mississippi territory (then claimed but barely settled by France). Law's Mississippi Company issued shares that promised vast profits from colonial trade. He could also issue paper money — France's first — backed by these expected profits. Shares went from 500 livres to 18,000 livres in a year. Then collapsed. Law fled France in disguise.

In England, the South Sea Company ran a parallel scheme involving trading rights to South America (largely fictional, since Spain controlled the region). Shares went from £128 to £1,050 over a few months in 1720. Then collapsed by 80%.

One of the South Sea Company's investors was Isaac Newton — yes, that Isaac Newton, the most rational mind of his age. He bought early, sold at a profit, watched the price keep climbing, bought back in at much higher prices, and was wiped out. He reportedly said: "I can calculate the motion of heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people."

The South Sea Bubble led to England's Bubble Act of 1720, which restricted the formation of joint-stock companies for a century. The collapse defined what "bubble" would mean for the next 300 years.

The Gold Standard: when paper meant gold

By the 1870s, the major industrial countries had converged on the Gold Standard: every unit of currency (the British pound, the French franc, the German mark, eventually the American dollar) was defined as a fixed weight of gold. You could walk into a bank, hand over your paper money, and demand the corresponding gold.

This system had clear virtues. Inflation was almost zero over decades — by 1914, the British pound bought the same as it had in 1870. International trade was easy: every currency was effectively the same currency, just measured in different denominations of gold. Governments couldn't easily run deficits, because they couldn't just print money. There was discipline.

It also had clear costs. Whole national economies were limited by how much gold was in their central bank vault. Economic shocks could not be cushioned. If a financial panic hit, the central bank couldn't print money to provide liquidity — it would have to let banks fail. Recessions were deeper and more brutal than what we tolerate today.

The Gold Standard worked, more or less, from about 1870 to 1914 — a period of remarkable global trade integration that some historians call the first globalization. Then the world went to war.

Weimar: when money becomes wallpaper

World War I shattered the Gold Standard. Countries needed to spend vastly more than their gold reserves allowed. They suspended convertibility — paper money would no longer be exchangeable for gold for the duration of the war. They printed.

After the war, the victors imposed massive reparations on Germany under the Treaty of Versailles. To pay reparations, Germany had to acquire foreign currency. To acquire foreign currency, it sold its own currency on international markets. The mark fell. As it fell, Germany needed to print more marks to buy the same amount of foreign currency. The mark fell faster. Germany printed more.

In January 1922, one U.S. dollar bought 192 German marks. By November 1923, it bought 4,200,000,000,000 marks. That is four trillion two hundred billion. Prices in Germany doubled every few days.

Eyewitness
Wheelbarrows of cash
Berlin, 1923

Workers were paid twice a day. Their first instinct upon receiving wages was to run — literally run — to the shops, because by evening the same money would buy half as much. Restaurants stopped printing menus; prices were chalked on boards and updated hourly. A cup of coffee that cost 5,000 marks at the start of a meal might cost 8,000 at the end. People burned banknotes for heat — it was cheaper than buying wood. Savings of a lifetime — pensions, war bonds, bank accounts — were wiped out in months. A middle-class family's life savings could buy a loaf of bread, then a stamp, then nothing.

The political consequences are well known. The economic destruction of the German middle class created the conditions in which authoritarianism could take root a decade later. This was inflation as a national catastrophe, not just an inconvenience.

Weimar is the extreme version of what happens when the social contract behind money breaks. It's the case study every central banker keeps in mind. It's also the reason Germans, more than most Europeans, remain instinctively skeptical of monetary loosening even today — a national memory that has shaped European Central Bank policy for decades.

1929: the crash that changed everything

The 1920s were the original boom decade. American stocks rose 400% between 1923 and 1929. Ordinary people, for the first time in history, were borrowing money to buy shares — "buying on margin," sometimes putting down just 10% of the purchase price. Shoeshine boys gave stock tips. (This is where the cliché comes from.)

On October 24, 1929 — Black Thursday — the market dropped 11% at the open. On October 28, it dropped 13%. On October 29 (Black Tuesday), another 12%. Margin calls forced leveraged buyers to dump shares to repay loans, accelerating the fall. By 1932, the market had lost 89% of its peak value. It would not return to its 1929 high for 25 years.

The crash itself was bad. What followed was worse. As stocks collapsed, banks that had loaned money for stock purchases failed. As banks failed, depositors lost their savings. Surviving banks stopped lending. Businesses, unable to get credit, laid off workers. Unemployment hit 25% in the U.S. by 1933. Global trade collapsed.

The lesson policymakers drew — and which would shape monetary policy ever since — was that the Federal Reserve had failed to do its job. Faced with bank failures, the Fed had stood by, letting the money supply contract by a third. A future Fed chairman, Ben Bernanke, would later say to Milton Friedman on the latter's 90th birthday: "You're right, we did it. We're very sorry. But thanks to you, we won't do it again."

Hold onto that promise. We'll see it cashed in 2008 and 2020.

Bretton Woods: gold's last stand

In July 1944, with World War II still raging, delegates from 44 Allied nations met at the Mount Washington Hotel in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, to design the post-war financial order. The Soviet Union sent observers, then declined to ratify.

The system they designed was a compromise. Each country's currency would be pegged to the U.S. dollar at a fixed rate. The U.S. dollar, in turn, would be redeemable in gold at $35 per ounce. The U.S. effectively underwrote the entire global monetary system with its enormous gold reserves at Fort Knox.

Why did everyone agree? Because the U.S. emerged from WWII with roughly two-thirds of the world's gold and a manufacturing economy that hadn't been bombed. The dollar was the only currency anyone trusted. Bretton Woods institutionalized that trust.

The system also created the International Monetary Fund (to help countries with balance-of-payments problems) and what became the World Bank (to fund reconstruction and development). Both institutions are still with us today.

For about 25 years — through the 1950s and 1960s — Bretton Woods worked. Trade flourished. Economies grew. Inflation stayed low. It was, in retrospect, a golden age for ordinary workers in the developed world.

Then it broke.

August 15, 1971: the day money changed forever

The Bretton Woods system had a fatal weakness. The U.S. printed dollars to fund the Vietnam War and Lyndon Johnson's domestic programs. As more dollars circulated globally than the U.S. had gold to back, foreign governments started getting nervous. France, in particular, began sending dollars back to America and demanding gold in return. Charles de Gaulle famously called the dollar's privileged role an "exorbitant privilege."

By summer 1971, the run on America's gold had become dangerous. Fort Knox was being drained. President Richard Nixon faced a choice: massively contract the U.S. economy to defend the gold price, or break the promise.

On Sunday evening, August 15, 1971, Nixon went on national television and announced — among a package of measures — that the United States would "temporarily" suspend the convertibility of the dollar into gold. The temporary suspension has now lasted 55 years and counting.

This is the single most important event in modern monetary history. From August 15, 1971, the U.S. dollar — and within a few years, every major currency on earth — was backed by exactly nothing physical. It was backed by trust. By government decree. By the implicit understanding that everyone would keep accepting it.

The moment to remember

Every economic intuition built on the previous 2,500 years of monetary history — that money is somehow connected to a real, physical thing — became technically wrong on August 15, 1971. We have now spent 55 years living in a world that has never before existed: a world of pure fiat money, untethered to anything but collective belief. The fact that you've grown up in this world doesn't make it normal. It's an unprecedented experiment.

Within months, currencies started floating against each other freely. Exchange rates, which had been fixed for a generation, now moved by the minute. Gold, freed from its $35-per-ounce ceiling, rose to $850 within a decade. Inflation, which had been mild, took off.

The 1970s: stagflation and Volcker's medicine

The 1970s were a disaster for the developed world's economies. Two oil shocks (1973 and 1979) sent energy prices through the roof. Wage-price spirals took hold: workers, seeing their real wages fall, demanded raises; companies, facing higher costs, raised prices; workers demanded more raises. Inflation in the U.S. peaked at 14% in 1980. In the UK, 25%.

What was unprecedented was that this happened at the same time as economic stagnation and high unemployment. Economic textbooks of the time had taught that you got either inflation (when the economy was hot) or unemployment (when the economy was cold) — not both. This new disease was christened stagflation.

In August 1979, Jimmy Carter appointed Paul Volcker as chairman of the Federal Reserve. Volcker, a 6-foot-7 chain-smoking economist, decided to do something brutal. He would raise interest rates until inflation broke, regardless of the economic damage.

The Fed's benchmark rate hit 20% in 1981. Mortgage rates touched 18%. The U.S. economy collapsed into a deep recession. Unemployment rose to 10.8%. Farmers drove tractors to Washington to protest. Builders sent Volcker pieces of wood from houses that couldn't be sold.

But it worked. By 1983, U.S. inflation had fallen from 14% to 3%. Volcker had killed inflation, and the seeds were sown for the next 25 years of falling interest rates — and rising asset prices.

1985–2008: the great moderation, the great inflation of assets

From the mid-1980s through 2007, the developed world enjoyed what economists now call the Great Moderation: falling interest rates, low and stable inflation, and rising asset prices almost everywhere. Stocks went up. Bonds went up (because rates were falling). Real estate went up.

This wasn't an accident. The Fed under Alan Greenspan (1987–2006) developed what became known as the "Greenspan Put": whenever financial markets wobbled, the Fed would cut interest rates and inject liquidity. The 1987 crash, the 1998 Long-Term Capital Management blow-up, the 2000 dot-com crash, the 9/11 aftermath — every shock was met with cheaper money.

This had two effects. First, it dampened recessions and crises in real time, which everyone could see and applaud. Second, it gradually inflated asset prices to levels that, in retrospect, weren't supported by underlying fundamentals — which most people couldn't see, or didn't want to.

Japan provided the warning. After a spectacular property and stock bubble in the late 1980s — at its peak, the grounds of the Imperial Palace in Tokyo were notionally worth more than all the real estate in California combined — Japan's bubble collapsed in 1990. The Nikkei stock index, which had touched 39,000 in 1989, would not see those levels again for 35 years. Japan entered a period of deflation and stagnant growth that has shaped its economy ever since.

The lesson — that monetary policy could inflate asset bubbles that take decades to deflate — was available. Most policymakers ignored it.

2008 and Greece: the credit machine breaks

The 2008 Global Financial Crisis was, at its core, a fractional reserve banking failure on a planetary scale. American banks had made hundreds of billions in subprime mortgage loans — loans to borrowers who couldn't really afford them — and then packaged these loans into securities (called CDOs and MBSs) and sold them to investors around the world. Rating agencies, paid by the banks themselves, had stamped these securities AAA.

When U.S. house prices started falling in 2006–2007, the subprime borrowers defaulted. The "AAA" securities turned out to be worth far less than promised. Banks holding them suddenly didn't know which of their assets were good and which were worthless. They stopped lending to each other. The global credit system seized up.

Lehman Brothers, the fourth-largest U.S. investment bank, failed on September 15, 2008. The contagion threatened to take down the entire global financial system. Central banks responded with the largest coordinated bailout in history: trillions of dollars in liquidity injected into the banking system, near-zero interest rates, and a new tool called quantitative easing — central banks creating money out of thin air to buy government bonds and other assets, in order to suppress long-term interest rates.

The U.S. and most major economies avoided a 1930s-style depression. They recovered, slowly. The cost of the rescue was an explosion of central bank balance sheets — the Fed's grew from $900 billion to $4.5 trillion within a few years — and the entrenchment of near-zero interest rates as the new normal.

Closer to home
The Greek crisis
Athens · 2010–2018

The 2008 crisis exposed a different problem in Europe: the eurozone. Greece, like most southern European countries, had benefited enormously from joining the euro in 2001 — suddenly being able to borrow at German interest rates rather than at the much higher rates the drachma had commanded. The Greek government, and Greek households, took advantage.

When the 2008 crisis hit and growth collapsed, Greece's debt-to-GDP ratio became unsustainable. In late 2009, the new Greek government revealed that the previous government had been hiding the true size of the deficit. Investors panicked. Greek government bond yields, which had been near German levels, exploded to over 30%.

Greece could not devalue its currency to ease the pain — it didn't have its own currency anymore. The only path was austerity: massive cuts in government spending, pension reductions, tax hikes, mass layoffs. Greek GDP fell by 25% — comparable to the U.S. Great Depression. Youth unemployment reached 60%. A generation of Greeks emigrated.

The crisis was eventually "resolved" through a series of bailouts from the IMF and EU, conditional on continuing austerity. The underlying problem — that a single currency requires either a single fiscal authority or constant transfers between richer and poorer regions — was papered over rather than solved. It will be back.

2020: the great printing

In March 2020, as COVID-19 spread and economies shut down, global financial markets cratered in the fastest crash in history. The S&P 500 lost 34% in 33 days. Oil prices briefly went negative — sellers paying buyers to take oil off their hands, because storage was full.

The response was unprecedented even by 2008 standards. The Federal Reserve cut rates to zero and began creating money at a pace never seen before. In a single year, the U.S. money supply (the M2 measure) grew by 26% — the largest one-year increase in American history. The U.S. government sent stimulus checks directly to households. The European Central Bank, the Bank of Japan, and the Bank of England all did versions of the same thing.

For about 18 months, the strategy seemed to work miraculously. Markets recovered within months. Stocks, crypto, real estate, art, classic cars — every asset class went up at once. Many ordinary people experienced their first taste of "investing wealth" — though almost entirely as a function of asset price inflation, not productive economic gains.

Then, in 2022, the bill arrived. Inflation, which had been dormant for 30 years, returned with force. U.S. inflation hit 9% in mid-2022 — the highest in 40 years. European inflation, supercharged by the Ukraine war's effect on energy prices, hit 10%. Central banks, having created vast amounts of new money in 2020–2021, now had to reverse course aggressively, raising interest rates at the fastest pace in decades.

The aftermath of 2020 is still being worked out. Some lessons are already clear: the post-1971 fiat money system has enormous capacity to respond to crises by simply creating money, but every such response shifts wealth and exacerbates inequality (those who own assets get richer; those holding cash get poorer), and the cumulative effect over decades is structural fragility.

Where we are now (May 2026)

Take stock of the landscape you're entering:

  • Money is fiat. There is no longer any physical thing it represents. Its value depends entirely on collective trust, enforced by governments through legal tender laws and tax collection in that currency.
  • Central banks have enormous power and use it aggressively. Their balance sheets are vastly larger than they were 20 years ago. They have demonstrated, repeatedly, that they will create money when financial stress demands it.
  • Inflation is back as a real concern after a 30-year vacation. Whether it stabilizes at 2-3% or proves more persistent is unknown. The risk of holding cash is the highest it has been in decades.
  • Government debt across developed economies is at peacetime highs. The historical resolutions of such debt levels are: rapid growth (rare), default (rare in countries with their own currencies), or inflation (common). Bet accordingly.
  • The 40-year bull market in bonds (caused by falling interest rates) is over. The 2008–2020 era of free money is over. What replaces it is being figured out in real time.
  • New forms of money are emerging. Central Bank Digital Currencies are being designed by every major central bank. Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies, whatever you think of them, represent a real experiment in non-state money for the first time in centuries.
  • Geopolitical tensions are rising in ways that affect money — sanctions, the weaponization of the dollar, fragmenting trade flows, gold reserves being repatriated.

This is the world into which you're investing. It's not the stable, low-inflation, falling-rates world your parents invested into. The basics still apply — compounding, diversification, time horizon — but the assumptions baked into a lot of conventional advice (that bonds always cushion stocks, that 60/40 portfolios always work, that inflation is dead) deserve fresh scrutiny.

"History doesn't repeat, but it rhymes." — attributed to Mark Twain

Now that you know where the system came from, you can read the next chapter with the right kind of skepticism: not the conspiratorial kind that thinks money is a scam, but the historical kind that knows monetary systems are human constructions, designed under pressure, full of trade-offs, periodically remade. The one you're living inside today is the latest version — not the final one.
