Human Decoded · Chapter 04

Writing, Cities
& the Birth of Mass Manipulation

Writing was not invented to record poetry. It was invented to record debts. And from that first clay tablet, a technology for controlling minds at scale was slowly, systematically built.

The first writing systems, developed independently in Mesopotamia and Egypt around 3,200 BCE, were not used for literature or philosophy. They were used for accounting — recording grain stocks, tax obligations, and labour rosters. Writing was, at its origin, a tool of state control: a mechanism for tracking who owed what to whom, across distances and time spans that exceeded human memory.

But the technology of encoding meaning in durable symbols — once developed — could do something far more powerful. It could transmit authority claims across time. A living king could command, threaten, and reward those in his immediate presence. But with writing, a dead king's decrees could still enforce compliance. A god who had never been seen could speak with permanent, authoritative voice. The past could be edited. History could be rewritten. Propaganda could be stamped on monuments that would outlast every person who remembered the truth.

The 360° Context: The First Cities

🌍 Scale

Uruk, the world's first major city (~3,400 BCE), had perhaps 80,000 residents at its peak. For a species whose social brain was calibrated for 150, this was a cognitive shock. Strangers everywhere. No personal reputation to rely on.

🏛️ Political

Cities required new governance: taxation, law, enforcement, bureaucracy. These needed legitimation beyond personal authority. Writing enabled both the administration and the ideology that justified it.

📡 Information

Writing created the first information asymmetry at scale. The literate minority — priests, scribes, administrators — controlled the interpretation of records, laws, and sacred texts. The illiterate majority had to trust intermediaries.

🧠 Cognitive

Reading and writing rewire the brain — literally. Literate and illiterate people process visual information differently. Literacy increases linear, sequential thinking and decreases oral/narrative dependence. But it also creates new vulnerabilities to written authority.

💰 Economic

Writing enabled long-distance contracts, credit, complex property ownership, and taxation at scale. The economy grew. But so did the gap between those who understood the system and those who didn't.

⚔️ Military

Writing enabled military logistics at empire scale. You could coordinate armies across hundreds of miles, record supply inventories, and send sealed orders. Empire became possible. So did propaganda justifying empire.

The First Propaganda Machine

The Egyptian pharaohs were among the first rulers to systematically exploit written and visual communication for what we would today call propaganda. The massive temples, obelisks, and tomb paintings of ancient Egypt were not primarily religious devotion — they were political communication at monumental scale. The consistent messages: the pharaoh is divine, the pharaoh is invincible, the existing order is eternal and cosmic, and resistance is not merely futile but sacrilegious.

This worked because it was perfectly calibrated to the evolved psychology of its audience. Authority bias: the most powerful being in the known world was proclaiming the message. Social proof: everyone else accepted it. Narrative: the message was embedded in compelling stories about gods, heroes, and cosmic battles. Fear: the consequences of defiance were made vivid. And repetition: the same messages appeared on every available surface, in every village temple, in every administrative centre.

None of the psychological mechanisms exploited by ancient Egyptian propaganda are different from those exploited by modern political communication. Only the media have changed.

Definition — Narrative Framing

Framing is the process of selecting and emphasising certain aspects of a situation while downplaying others, in order to promote a particular interpretation. Every act of communication involves framing — there is no "neutral" description of complex events. What propaganda does is use framing systematically and at scale to make one interpretation feel natural, inevitable, and obvious, while making alternative interpretations feel foreign, dangerous, or absurd. Framing operates below conscious awareness in most people most of the time. It is the primary tool of every political communication system in history.

Alexander the Great: The First Modern Propagandist

Real Story — Alexander's Information Strategy

Alexander the Great (356–323 BCE) was not merely a military genius. He was the first ruler we know of who systematically managed his own image and narrative in real time across a vast empire. His court historian, Callisthenes, was tasked not with recording events accurately but with constructing a compelling account of Alexander as descended from Hercules and Achilles, divinely chosen, invincible, and civilising. Callisthenes' accounts were read aloud to troops before battles. They were circulated in conquered territories. They shaped how Greeks, Macedonians, Persians, and Egyptians understood what Alexander was and what he represented.

Alexander also understood the power of symbolic communication. When he visited the oracle at Siwa and was reportedly declared the son of Zeus-Ammon, he made sure the story circulated. When he conquered Egypt, he performed the pharaonic rituals and was depicted in temple carvings as pharaoh. He did not force Persians to become Greek — he adopted Persian court customs, wore Persian dress, and presented himself as a legitimate heir to both traditions. This was not cultural sensitivity. It was sophisticated identity management: presenting a face to each audience that maximised their acceptance of his authority.

The techniques Alexander used — managing personal mythology, co-opting local religious legitimacy, controlling the historical record, and using emotional narrative to motivate followers — are the precise techniques used by every successful populist leader and major corporation in the 21st century. The technology of delivery has changed beyond recognition. The psychological architecture being targeted has not changed at all.

The Byzantine Information System: Greece as a Case Study

The Byzantine Empire — the eastern successor to Rome, centred in Constantinople (modern Istanbul) and heavily culturally Greek — developed what may have been the most sophisticated information management system of the pre-modern world. For over a thousand years, the Byzantine state maintained control over a multi-ethnic, multi-religious empire through a combination of bureaucratic complexity, ceremonial spectacle, theological legitimation, and systematic control of the narrative about imperial authority.

The Byzantine church and state were so thoroughly intertwined that challenging the emperor's authority and challenging the Orthodox faith were essentially the same act. This "caesaropapism" — the fusion of political and religious authority — was not unique to Byzantium, but Byzantium refined it to extraordinary sophistication. The elaborate court ceremonies, the sacred iconography of the emperor, the positioning of Constantinople as the "New Jerusalem" — all served to make imperial authority feel not like a human political arrangement but like the expression of divine cosmic order.

The legacy of Byzantine information culture in modern Greece is real and documented. Studies of Greek political culture consistently find unusually high levels of political cynicism combined with unusually strong attachment to Orthodox religious identity — a combination that makes perfect sense as the inheritance of a political culture in which institutional power was always suspect (because it was always extractive) but religious identity provided the stable foundation of collective belonging.

The Cognitive Load of Urban Life

Cities introduced a new form of cognitive stress: information overload. A hunter-gatherer processes perhaps hundreds of social signals per day, from people they know personally. A resident of ancient Athens, medieval Constantinople, or modern Athens processes thousands of signals from strangers, institutions, and competing authorities.

When the brain is overloaded, it defaults to heuristics — the cognitive shortcuts we discussed in Chapter 01. And those heuristics are systematically exploitable. Authority bias: defer to the most impressive-seeming authority. Social proof: do what everyone else seems to be doing. Availability: worry about the most vivid threat. These shortcuts are efficient when information is limited and trust networks are small. In an information-rich urban environment, they become vulnerabilities.

Insight — The Origin of Advertising

Ancient Pompeii has given archaeologists something extraordinary: the remains of the world's first clearly documented commercial advertising campaign. Wine merchants painted their products on walls, with illustrated jugs and enthusiastic claims about quality. Gladiatorial schools posted fight cards. Bakers marked their bread with stamps identifying the bakery. The basic psychological mechanisms — brand association, social proof, scarcity signalling — were already in place 2,000 years ago. The difference between Pompeii's walls and a modern Instagram feed is entirely one of scale and sophistication, not of underlying psychology. Every technique in modern advertising was discovered empirically, over millennia, by people who noticed that it worked.

Authority Bias in Action: The Milgram Studies Revisited

We mentioned the Milgram experiments in Chapter 01 as a demonstration of authority bias. It's worth dwelling on them here in the context of large-scale manipulation. Stanley Milgram's 1961 experiments showed that approximately 65% of ordinary Americans would administer what they believed were potentially fatal electric shocks to strangers, simply because a man in a white coat told them to. The experiment was designed in response to the Holocaust — specifically to understand how ordinary Germans had participated in systematic mass murder without personal malice toward victims.

The answer Milgram found was not ideology, not cruelty, not unusual psychology. It was obedience — the same evolved deference to authority that we discussed in Chapter 01. The implication is deeply uncomfortable: most people, in most conditions, will perform acts they would consider monstrous if instructed by a sufficiently legitimate-seeming authority. This is not a German characteristic. It is a human characteristic.

The modern application is direct. Large-scale atrocities — genocide, ethnic cleansing, industrial persecution — do not require persuading millions of people to hate. They require creating a legitimate-seeming authority that normalises participation, creating a narrative that reframes victims as a threat, and providing a social environment in which non-participation is the deviant choice. Writing, and later broadcast media, made the creation of such environments possible at civilisational scale.

The Printing Press: Mass Manipulation's First Platform Change

Johannes Gutenberg's movable type press, developed around 1440, did to information what agriculture did to calories: it created surplus. Within 50 years, millions of books circulated in Europe where thousands had existed before. Within 100 years, Martin Luther's 95 Theses had ignited a Reformation that shattered the Catholic Church's information monopoly and ultimately killed millions of people in religious wars.

The printing press demonstrates a pattern that repeats with every new information technology: when a monopoly on information distribution is broken, there is first an explosion of diversity (the Reformation, the Enlightenment, the scientific revolution) and then a new battle for control of the dominant narrative. The Catholic Church lost its information monopoly. Nation-states and their associated newspapers gained a new one. And the psychological mechanisms that made people susceptible to religious authority transferred smoothly to national, ethnic, and ideological authority.

The internet was supposed to democratise information again. In the next chapters, we'll see what actually happened instead.
