Tech,
rewinding the tape.

The history of technology, read through three lenses: what each invention did to money, what it did to power, and what it did to the texture of ordinary days.

Eight chapters · ~6 hours reading · Built for the curious

Most histories of technology are lists — this invention, then that one, ever upward. This one tries to be something else: a causal story. Each chapter takes a stretch of time and asks the same three questions of whatever got built in it.

What did it do to money — to how value was stored, moved, and captured? What did it do to power — to who could coerce, persuade, or escape whom? And what did it do to daily life — to the ordinary texture of waking up, working, eating, and dying? Read that way, a stone blade and a language model turn out to rhyme.

The chapters cover three million years in eight parts, from the first sharpened stone to the AI system you may be reading this on. They build on each other — the patterns established in Chapter 01 are still running in Chapter 08 — but each can also be read on its own.

Why this exists

I kept reaching for technology metaphors to explain other things — money, institutions, power, why the present feels the way it does — and realised I didn't actually have the history straight. Not the list of inventions and dates, but the causal story: why things happened when they did, who benefited, what conditions made them possible. So I wrote it down, the long way, to fix that. This is the result.

Start at Chapter 01 and read through, or jump in wherever the era interests you most. Either way works. The patterns become visible over the whole arc, but each chapter holds on its own.
